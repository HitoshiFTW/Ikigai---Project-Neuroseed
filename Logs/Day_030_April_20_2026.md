## I. Architecture Consolidation: Stabilising the Day 29 Adaptive Loop

The Day 29 architecture established a consequence-bound cognition loop: reasoning incurs energy, commits to one action, is judged by outcome, and is conditioned by memory. This loop is necessary for adaptive behaviour, but it is not sufficient for stable adaptive behaviour. A loop without verification can drift without detection. A loop without load regulation can deplete its own substrate and eliminate the conditions for further adaptation. A loop without confidence calibration will, in environments where prediction errors are systematically biased, converge on consistently overconfident commitments that the outcome layer will repeatedly penalise but that the confidence layer will not correct. A loop without bounded policy shaping will exhibit action distributions that are purely reactive to per-tick signals without any accumulated temporal structure.

Day 30 addresses each of these structural absences in order. Pack 1 introduces long-horizon measurement of the loop's own output, producing a verdict classification that allows the architecture to determine whether prediction errors are decaying, rewards are improving, energy is stable, and the fallback mechanism is exercised. Pack 2 introduces load-aware constraint scaling, reducing reasoning complexity dynamically when metabolic strain signals indicate that full deliberation is unsustainable. Pack 3 introduces error-driven confidence correction, computing an uncertainty estimate from the rolling distribution of prediction errors and applying it as a multiplicative discount to the base confidence signal. Pack 4 introduces a slow EMA-based policy bias per action type, driven by the dopamine signal derived from the gap between actual reward and the EMA of prior rewards, bounded by a maximum effect of 0.10 on adjusted confidence.

The result is a hierarchy with four clearly delineated levels. Outcome credit and reality-based pruning from Day 29 remain the deepest layer: they are the primary arbiters of branch survival and prediction correctness. Memory-conditioned cognition sits above this, modulating confidence from episodic precedent. Confidence calibration from Pack 3 Day 30 applies a correction to the signal that memory and base reasoning produce jointly, discounting it by the organism's empirical uncertainty. The dopamine bias from Pack 4 Day 30 applies the smallest possible influence at the top of the chain, nudging adjusted confidence by at most ±0.10 in the direction of historically rewarded or penalised action types. No subsystem dominates. Outcome, memory, calibration, and bias now exist in a balanced hierarchy in which each layer's influence is bounded, transparent, and subordinate to the layers below it.

## II. Pack 1 -- Stability and Truth Audit

Pack 1 introduced the first long-horizon observer layer in NeuroSeed, measuring whether the closed adaptive loop established by Day 29 produces genuine behavioural improvement over time rather than producing locally consistent but globally degrading dynamics. The theoretical grounding draws on Sutton and Barto's (1998) convergence analysis of temporal difference learning, which establishes that TD prediction error must decay toward zero over time in a stationary environment for a system to be classified as learning rather than oscillating. Shannon's (1948) entropy measure applied to action-type distributions provides the canonical test for behavioural convergence: a system that has learned should exhibit lower entropy over its action distribution than one that is still exploring. Pearson's (1895) least-squares linear regression provides the slope statistic for trend detection. Ashby's (1956) Law of Requisite Variety supplies the stability criterion: a system is stable when its state variety remains bounded and its energy does not collapse or diverge. Pack 1 is strictly observer-only. No weights are modified, no actions influenced, no biological state written. All four classes read existing system attributes and produce derived metrics into their own bounded histories.

Four frozen constants govern Pack 1.

**Rule R1 (Frozen):** Observer-only -- zero upstream mutation in any of the four classes.

**Rule R2 (Frozen):** `HORIZONS = [50, 100, 200]`. All three window sizes are computed simultaneously as soon as accumulated history reaches each threshold. No intermediate horizon may be substituted or omitted.

**Rule R3 (Frozen):** `TREND_WINDOW = 50`. Slope computation uses exactly the last 50 ticks of absolute prediction error and reward data.

**Rule R6 (Frozen):** Verdict logic: `'stable'` requires simultaneously `error_trend < 0`, `reward_trend > 0`, `energy_stability = True`, and `fallback_rate > 0`. `'unstable'` is declared when `energy < ENERGY_FLOOR = 0.20` or `error_trend >= INSTABILITY_SLOPE_THRESHOLD = 0.002`. `'inconclusive'` covers all remaining cases including `n_records < MIN_RECORDS_FOR_VERDICT = 10`.

`LongHorizonSimulationRunner` collects the canonical per-tick record `{tick, predicted_EES, actual_reward, prediction_error, energy, fallback_used, selected_action}` into `deque(maxlen=256)`. Energy is read from `l23.energy` when the cortical compartment global is present in the waking loop, or approximated from `CognitiveMetabolicRegulator.efficiency_ratio / 2.0` otherwise. Aggregated statistics -- `mean_abs_error`, `mean_reward`, `mean_energy`, and `fallback_rate` -- are computed over each of the three horizon windows as soon as sufficient history has accumulated. These four aggregates constitute the ground-truth signal for the verdict layer, immune to per-tick noise.

`PredictionErrorTracker` computes four statistics over the last `TREND_WINDOW = 50` ticks: `mean_abs_error`, `error_trend_slope` (negative value indicates prediction error improving), `variance` (measuring stability of the error signal), and `reward_trend_slope` (positive value indicates outcomes improving over time). The slope computation uses the exact Pearson formula: `slope = sum((i - x_mean) × (y_i - y_mean)) / sum((i - x_mean)^2)`, with a `1e-12` denominator guard.

`BehaviorConvergenceAnalyzer` tracks a lifetime `_action_counts` dictionary preserved across `to_dict`/`from_dict` round-trips, and computes the frozen Rule R5 formula: `convergence_score = dominant_frequency / max(entropy, 1e-6)`. A converging system exhibits rising `dominant_frequency`, falling `entropy`, and therefore a rising `convergence_score`. The `memory_influence` attribute measures the rolling mean of `|memory_adjusted_confidence - binding_confidence|` per tick, quantifying the Pack 4 Day 29 memory layer's active contribution to confidence adjustment above the base reasoning signal.

`SystemStabilityMonitor` aggregates signals from the three prior observer classes each waking tick to produce `audit_result` containing the verdict, the supporting statistics, and the energy stability flag. The verdict is re-evaluated every waking tick from the latest statistics and is not latched. In constant-input synthetic tests, the verdict is `'inconclusive'`, which is the correct classification: no observable trend exists when inputs do not vary. In real-organism operation where prediction errors decay through learning, the verdict transitions to `'stable'` as the adaptive loop converges. Validation: **18/18 PASS** (`experiments/day30_pack1_stability_audit.py`), covering one-record-per-tick, deque bound at 256, horizon population threshold (horizon absent at 49 records, present at 50), all four PET metrics present, negative slope on decreasing-PE series (slope = -0.010000), positive slope on increasing-reward series (slope = +0.010000), convergence_score growth (early = 0.2081, late = 1.2230), entropy decay (2.0000 to 0.3976 on concentration), action_counts roundtrip, stable verdict on healthy 50-tick run, unstable verdict on energy-collapse scenario, inconclusive with fewer than 10 records, all three horizons [50, 100, 200] populated at 200 ticks, zero Day 28-29 drift (10 constants verified), sleeping guard, and round-trip fidelity for all four classes.

## III. Pack 2 -- Overload Control and Reactive Efficiency Mode

Pack 2 introduced strain-aware cognition control, enabling the system to reduce reasoning complexity dynamically under metabolic overload without collapsing into unconditional fallback or ceasing deliberation entirely. The theoretical grounding draws on Kahneman's (2011) dual-process framework, which established that System 2 deliberation is energetically expensive and is naturally suppressed under fatigue and resource scarcity, leaving System 1 reactive inference as the computationally cheaper default. Yerkes and Dodson's (1908) inverted-U law demonstrates that cognitive performance peaks at intermediate arousal and degrades under both under- and over-stimulation, providing the empirical anchor for the multi-level threshold design. Selye's (1950) general adaptation syndrome formalises the three-phase response to sustained stressor exposure -- alarm, resistance, and exhaustion -- and motivates the distinction between soft strain (resistance phase, still functional) and overload (exhaustion phase, degraded mode required). Pack 2 outputs are advisory control signals throughout. No upstream Day 28-29 class is written by any Pack 2 component. The pack recommends constraint adjustments; enforcement occurs through downstream chain consumers reading the advisory outputs.

Six frozen constants govern Pack 2.

**Rule R1 (Frozen):** `SOFT_THRESHOLD = 0.60`, `OVERLOAD_THRESHOLD = 0.75`. These two values define the three-way strain classification and may not be dynamically adjusted by any downstream class.

**Rule R2 (Frozen):** Soft-strain depth and retrieval caps: `recommended_chain_depth = 3`, `recommended_retrieval_count = 3`.

**Rule R4 (Frozen):** Overload depth and retrieval caps: `recommended_chain_depth = 1`, `recommended_retrieval_count = 2`.

**Rule R5 (Frozen):** `OVERLOAD_TICKS = 5`. Recovery triggers only when `_consecutive_overload >= 5` AND `energy_declining = True` AND `reward_improving = False` simultaneously.

`CognitiveLoadMonitor` reads `CognitiveMetabolicRegulator.strain_score` from Pack 2 Day 29 and classifies the organism's current metabolic state into one of three trend states: `'healthy'` when `strain < 0.60`, `'soft_strain'` when `0.60 <= strain < 0.75`, and `'overload'` when `strain >= 0.75`, with `overload_flag = True` in the last case. Energy trend is derived from the last 20 `LongHorizonSimulationRunner` tick records: if the mean energy of the more-recent half is lower than the mean of the earlier half by more than 0.01, `energy_declining = True`. `reward_improving` is `True` when `PredictionErrorTracker.reward_trend_slope > 0`.

`DynamicReasoningLimiter` maps the load classification to advisory depth and retrieval limits and to three boolean feature flags (`multi_hop_enabled`, `long_form_enabled`, `negotiation_enabled`). In `'healthy'` state all flags are `True` and limits are at their normal values (6 and 5). In `'soft_strain'` both limits drop to 3 per Rule R2. In `'overload'` limits drop to 1 and 2 per Rule R4 and all three flags become `False`. The limiter applies no direct writes to chain construction infrastructure; it exposes advisory attributes that downstream components read and honour.

`ReactiveEfficiencyController` maps the load state to an adjusted binding threshold: `0.60` in `'healthy'`, `0.65` in `'soft_strain'` (raised by `SOFT_BIAS = 0.05`), `0.70` in `'overload'` (raised by `OVERLOAD_BIAS = 0.10`). The threshold elevation makes binding harder under strain: only higher-confidence actions proceed in degraded mode, reducing the risk of committing to low-confidence deliberations during metabolic crisis. In `'overload'`, `disabled_features = ['multi_hop_inference', 'long_form_reasoning', 'negotiation_loops']` is populated.

`RecoveryStateManager` monitors cumulative overload exposure and emits a recovery pulse when the Rule R5 conditions are simultaneously satisfied. `recovery_triggered` is a single-tick pulse: `True` only on the first tick that all three conditions are met. `recovery_active` is a sustained flag that persists until `trend_state` returns to `'healthy'` or `'soft_strain'`, ensuring that recovery mode does not clear mid-overload. Both the consecutive overload counter and the `recovery_active` flag are preserved in `to_dict`/`from_dict` to prevent false state resets after save/restore cycles. Validation: **21/21 PASS** (`experiments/day30_pack2_overload_control.py`), covering CLM threshold boundaries (healthy at strain 0.40, soft_strain at 0.65, overload at 0.80, exact boundary transition at 0.75), energy_declining detection, DRL caps at all three states (6/5 in healthy, 3/3 in soft_strain, 1/2 in overload), DRL feature disabling in overload, REC threshold and bias at all three states, REC disabled_features list, RSM no trigger before 5 ticks (consecutive = 4), RSM trigger at tick 4 (5th consecutive overload tick), no trigger without energy decline, recovery_active clears on healthy state, 50-tick overload simulation (15 healthy, 15 soft-strain, 20 overload ticks, threshold range [0.60, 0.70]), zero Day 28-29 drift (15 constants), sleeping guard, and round-trip fidelity for all four classes.

## IV. Pack 3 -- Confidence Calibration and Uncertainty Correction

Pack 3 introduced prediction error-based confidence correction, enabling the organism to adjust the trust it places in its own reasoning in proportion to its recent history of prediction accuracy. The theoretical grounding draws on Lichtenstein and Fischhoff's (1977) calibration research, which established that subjective confidence systematically diverges from empirical accuracy and that well-calibrated agents are those whose stated confidence tracks observed frequency. Guo et al.'s (2017) finding that modern deep networks are overconfident and that linear scaling of the raw confidence signal restores calibration provides the architectural motivation for Pack 3's multiplicative correction approach. Fischler and Firschein's (1987) treatment of uncertainty as a first-class signal in reasoning systems establishes the principle that uncertainty must be propagated explicitly through the decision pipeline rather than suppressed or averaged away. Pack 3 does not modify upstream reasoning chains or binding thresholds directly. It corrects the confidence signal that those components produce, and biases the fallback controller in proportion to the magnitude of the correction.

Five frozen constants govern Pack 3.

**Rule R1 (Frozen):** `ERROR_WINDOW = 20`. The rolling window for absolute prediction error statistics is exactly 20 ticks.

**Rule R2 (Frozen):** `mean_error = mean(|prediction_error|)` over the last 20 ticks.

**Rule R3 (Frozen):** `uncertainty = mean_error × 0.60 + variance × 0.40`, clamped `[0, 1]`. The 60/40 weighting overindexes on systematic miscalibration (high mean) relative to volatile-but-centred error (high variance alone).

**Rule R4 (Frozen):** `calibrated_confidence = base_confidence × (1 − uncertainty)`.

**Rule R5 (Frozen):** `confidence_floor = 0.10`. The calibrated signal is clamped to `[0.10, 1.0]`, preserving the organism's capacity to act under even critical uncertainty.

`ConfidenceErrorTracker` maintains a rolling window of `ERROR_WINDOW = 20` absolute prediction errors, computing `mean_error`, `error_variance`, and `error_trend` (least-squares slope of the `|pe|` sequence, negative indicating calibration improving) each waking tick. The `pe_window` deque is preserved in `to_dict`/`from_dict` to allow exact restoration of rolling statistics across state save/restore cycles, avoiding discontinuous recalibration artefacts after organism restart.

`UncertaintyEstimator` computes `uncertainty_score` from the Rule R3 formula, clamped to `[0, 1]`, and emits `uncertainty_reason` as a four-level ASCII label: `low_uncertainty` when score `< 0.25`, `moderate_uncertainty` when `< 0.50`, `high_uncertainty` when `< 0.75`, and `critical_uncertainty` when `>= 0.75`.

`ConfidenceCalibrator` applies the Rule R4 formula, sourcing `base_confidence` from `ActionBindingGate.binding_confidence` (Pack 1 Day 29). `calibration_delta = calibrated_confidence − base_confidence` is exposed as a signed measure of the correction magnitude. Two illustrative cases bound the operating envelope: with `base = 0.80` and `uncertainty = 0.50`, the result is `calibrated = 0.40`, a 0.40-unit downward correction reflecting substantial recent error; with `base = 0.20` and `uncertainty = 0.99`, the floor clamps the result to `calibrated = 0.10` rather than the arithmetic `0.002`, preserving minimal agency.

`FallbackDecisionController` maps `calibrated_confidence` to a discrete `decision_state` and a scalar `fallback_adjustment`. The three-level staircase implements Rule R6: `trust_reasoning` when `calibrated >= 0.60` with zero fallback adjustment; `mild_fallback` when `0.30 <= calibrated < 0.60` with `fallback_adjustment = 0.10`; `strong_fallback` when `calibrated < 0.30` with `fallback_adjustment = 0.25`. Recovery from strong or mild fallback is automatic: as prediction error decays, uncertainty drops, calibrated confidence rises, and the controller returns to `trust_reasoning` without any explicit reset signal. Validation: **32/32 PASS** (`experiments/day30_pack3_confidence_calibration.py`), covering single-update mean_error exactness, three-value window mean (0.400000), error_variance nonzero for varied input and near-zero for constant input, negative slope on decreasing-PE sequence, ERROR_WINDOW = 20 frozen (R1), R3 formula exact (unc = 0.40 for mean = 0.50 / var = 0.25), uncertainty clamped to 1.0 for extreme input, all four uncertainty_reason labels, R4 formula exact (cal = 0.40 for base = 0.80 / unc = 0.50), calibration_delta sign correct, confidence_floor = 0.10 enforced (cal = 0.10 for base = 0.20 / unc = 0.99), trust_reasoning state at cal >= 0.60, mild_fallback at cal = 0.45 (adj = 0.10), strong_fallback at cal = 0.15 (adj = 0.25), FALLBACK_THRESHOLD = 0.60 frozen (R6), sleeping guard across all four classes, zero Day 28-29 drift (14 constants), round-trip fidelity for all four classes, 50-tick simulation (high-PE phase: unc = 0.480, cal = 0.416, 25/25 fallback ticks; low-PE recovery phase: unc = 0.231, cal = 0.615, 13/25 trust-reasoning ticks), no permanent fallback collapse (trust_reasoning recovered with cal = 0.795 after 25 low-PE ticks), and deque bounded at 96.

## V. Pack 4 -- Dopamine-Coupled Policy Shaping (Stabilized)

Pack 4 introduced the first persistent policy bias mechanism in NeuroSeed history, translating accumulated prediction error signals into slow, bounded adjustments to action-type confidence without overriding outcome credit, uncertainty calibration, or fallback triggers. The theoretical grounding draws on Schultz's (1997) landmark finding that midbrain dopamine neurons fire in proportion to reward prediction error -- the gap between received and expected reward -- establishing that dopamine is a surprise signal rather than a reward signal per se. Rescorla and Wagner's (1972) weight update rule is directly instantiated in the PolicyBiasTable update formula: under accurate prediction the dopamine signal approaches zero, the EMA-weighted update is negligible, and existing bias values decay toward zero at the BIAS_DECAY rate, preventing accumulation from outdated associations. Graybiel's (2008) analysis of habit formation in the basal ganglia motivates the four-state habit classification and the suppression mechanism: repeated reinforcement produces increasingly stable and automatic execution, while repeated penalisation produces temporary deactivation of the associated channel. Dayan and Niv's (2008) unified framework for reinforcement learning in the brain establishes the hierarchical relationship between the immediate TD dopamine signal (Pack 4) and the long-horizon value estimates that Pack 1's `LongHorizonSimulationRunner` computes. Pack 4 is explicitly subordinate in this hierarchy. It shapes the final confidence signal only after outcome credit has judged the action, calibration has discounted overconfidence, and the fallback controller has classified the current epistemic state.

Eight frozen constants govern Pack 4.

**Rule R1 (Frozen):** `EMA_ALPHA = 0.10`, `EMA_DECAY = 0.90`. The EMA of prior rewards decays slowly, ensuring that a single outlier reward does not produce a sustained dopamine signal.

**Rule R2 (Frozen):** `BIAS_DECAY = 0.95`, `BIAS_LEARN = 0.05`. The policy bias for each action type decays at 0.95 per tick and absorbs the dopamine signal at rate 0.05. This 19:1 decay-to-learn ratio produces slow accumulation by design: 20 ticks of maximum dopamine (+1.0) yields a bias of approximately 0.32, not 1.0.

**Rule R3 (Frozen):** `MAX_BIAS_EFFECT = 0.10`. The final confidence adjustment is `adjusted = calibrated_confidence + bias × 0.10`, clamped to `[0, 1]`. The maximum possible effect on adjusted confidence from any bias value is ±0.10, irrespective of the magnitude of the accumulated bias.

**Rule R5 (Frozen):** `POSITIVE_THRESHOLD = +0.20`, `NEGATIVE_THRESHOLD = −0.20`. Habit states `'stable'` and `'suppressed'` are assigned only when bias has crossed these thresholds.

**Rule R6 (Frozen):** `SUPPRESS_STREAK = 3`. Suppression of an action type requires `bias <= −0.20` for at least three consecutive waking ticks.

`DopamineSignalIntegrator` computes `dopamine = actual_reward − EMA_reward`, sourcing `actual_reward` from `OutcomeEvaluator.actual_reward` (Pack 3 Day 29). The EMA is initialised at 0.50 to ensure the first tick produces a meaningful signal rather than an artificially large spike from a zero baseline. `dopamine_reason` provides three labels: `positive_surprise` when `dopamine > 0.10`, `negative_surprise` when `dopamine < −0.10`, and `expectation_met` otherwise. The EMA value is preserved across `to_dict`/`from_dict`, ensuring continuity of the surprise signal after state restoration.

`PolicyBiasTable` updates only the active action type's bias on each tick (sparse credit assignment), leaving all other action type biases to decay passively at the BIAS_DECAY rate. The active action type is identified from `ActionBindingGate.binding_path`. The 0.95/0.05 split means that biases accumulate slowly and dissipate relatively quickly when the action type is inactive -- implementing the biological principle that unused habits fade while active ones consolidate. The `bias_table` dictionary is preserved in `to_dict`/`from_dict` to maintain accumulated preferences across session boundaries.

`HabitStabilityTracker` maintains per-action positive and negative streak counters, classifying each action type into one of four states: `'emerging'` when bias is within the threshold interval `(−0.20, +0.20)` or when streaks have not accumulated sufficiently; `'stable'` when bias `>= 0.20` with active positive streak; `'dominant'` when bias `>= 0.50`; and `'suppressed'` when the negative streak has reached `SUPPRESS_STREAK = 3`. Suppression clears automatically when bias rises above `NEGATIVE_THRESHOLD`, providing self-correcting recovery without requiring any external reset signal. Both streak counters are preserved in `to_dict`/`from_dict` to prevent false suppression resets after state restoration.

`BiasAdjustedDecisionModulator` applies the Rule R3 formula, sourcing `calibrated_confidence` from `ConfidenceCalibrator` (Pack 3 Day 30) and the active action type's bias from `PolicyBiasTable`. The `FallbackDecisionController.decision_state` and `fallback_adjustment` are recorded in each tick's history entry but are not modified by the modulator, satisfying Rule R4 structurally: fallback logic and calibration are upstream constraints that the dopamine layer cannot override, only influence by adding at most ±0.10 to the confidence value those constraints produce. Validation: **35/35 PASS** (`experiments/day30_pack4_dopamine_policy.py`), covering R1 formula exact (da = 0.300 for reward = 0.80 / EMA = 0.50), EMA update exact (ema = 0.530 after first tick), dopamine clamped to ±1, all three reason labels, slow accumulation confirmed (bias = 0.321 after 20 ticks of maximum dopamine), sparse credit assignment (approach bias = 0.321 after 10 ticks; explore and withdraw biases = 0.000000), suppression after exactly 3 consecutive negative ticks (R6), stable and dominant states reachable at appropriate bias thresholds (R5), no suppression at 2 consecutive ticks, MAX_BIAS_EFFECT = 0.10 exact (adj = 0.750 for cal = 0.70 / bias = 0.50), FDC state preserved in record confirming no override (R4), confidence clamped to [0, 1], all 8 Pack 4 constants frozen (R1-R6), zero Day 28-30 drift (8 upstream constants), sleeping guard across all four classes, full round-trip fidelity, deque bounded at 96, and 100-tick behaviour stabilisation simulation: positive-reward phase (ticks 0-49) yields bias_end = +0.022 and mean adjusted confidence = 0.705; negative-reward phase (ticks 50-74) yields bias_end = −0.117 and mean adjusted confidence = 0.688; calibration remains dominant throughout (max adjusted confidence = 0.708, ceiling = 0.800); no suppression observed in the pure-positive phase.

## VI. EEIL Interpretation of the Day 30 Architecture

The Energy Efficiency Intelligence Law predicts that intelligence emerges as the minimisation of energy expenditure per successful outcome in systems operating under constraint. Day 30's four packs extend the Day 29 EEIL instantiation by adding the four regulatory functions that the law predicts a sufficiently mature adaptive system must acquire: verification, regulation, calibration, and bounded reinforcement.

Pack 1 implements EEIL's empirical validity requirement. The law's testability depends on the ability to measure whether a system is actually improving its energy-efficiency ratio over time. Without `LongHorizonSimulationRunner`, `PredictionErrorTracker`, and `SystemStabilityMonitor`, the Day 29 adaptive loop operates but cannot confirm whether it is converging. Pack 1 supplies the measurement infrastructure that distinguishes genuine efficiency gain (decaying prediction error, improving reward trend, stable energy, non-zero fallback rate) from superficial activity that produces local consistency without global improvement. The verdict classification `'stable'` is the first formal claim that the adaptive loop is working as the law predicts.

Pack 2 implements EEIL's constraint enforcement under strain. The law predicts that an efficient system will not continue to apply maximum deliberation resources when those resources are being consumed faster than they are replenished. The `DynamicReasoningLimiter` and `ReactiveEfficiencyController` enforce exactly this: under soft strain, chain depth is halved and retrieval is reduced; under overload, chain depth drops to 1 and non-essential deliberation modes are disabled. The system does not collapse and does not attempt to maintain full reasoning at unsustainable metabolic cost. It degrades in proportion to the strain signal, preserving the minimal deliberative capacity needed for continued function. This is EEIL's graceful degradation prediction confirmed architecturally.

Pack 3 implements EEIL's elimination of overconfident inefficiency. The law identifies overconfidence as a specific form of inefficiency: a system that assigns high confidence to actions that produce low reward is misallocating its commitment and metabolic expenditure. The formula `calibrated_confidence = base_confidence × (1 − uncertainty)` is a direct application of the EEIL principle that confidence must track accuracy: high recent prediction error produces high uncertainty, which produces a multiplicative discount on base confidence, which reduces the probability of committing a high-energy deep deliberation to an action whose historical prediction quality does not warrant the cost. Intelligence is now regulated by both cost and correctness.

Pack 4 implements EEIL's bounded reinforcement prediction. The law does not predict that an efficient system will be stateless between trials; it predicts that a system will accumulate weak structural biases toward action types with consistently positive prediction error histories. These biases must be slow, bounded, and subordinate to outcome credit, because a system that allows reinforcement to dominate outcome feedback will degenerate toward monoculture -- the A3 degeneration pattern identified in the EEIL cross-environment experiments of Days 20-21. The `MAX_BIAS_EFFECT = 0.10` ceiling and the `BIAS_LEARN = 0.05` learning rate jointly enforce exactly this subordination: the dopamine layer can accumulate preferences, but those preferences cannot override the outcome-credit and calibration layers that govern the architecture below it.

The joint operation of the four packs produces a system in which intelligence is regulated from four orthogonal directions simultaneously: it is measured (Pack 1), constrained (Pack 2), corrected (Pack 3), and slowly shaped (Pack 4). The organism is not merely adaptive; it is self-correcting, load-aware, uncertainty-conscious, and habit-forming -- all within the bounds that EEIL prescribes.

## VII. Emergent Behaviour Observations

Several behaviourally significant patterns emerged during the Day 30 validation simulations that merit documentation.

The Pack 3 50-tick simulation demonstrated an asymmetry in the speed of confidence degradation versus recovery that was anticipated theoretically but is worth documenting precisely. During the high-PE phase (ticks 0-24, reward = 0.80, EMA ≈ 0.50), the error window saturated rapidly: by tick 20, all 20 positions in the `pe_window` deque contained absolute errors near 0.30, producing `uncertainty ≈ 0.48` and `calibrated_confidence ≈ 0.416`. The fallback controller was in `mild_fallback` state for all 25 high-PE ticks. During the low-PE recovery phase (ticks 25-49, reward = 0.05), the error window did not clear instantly: the older high-PE records remained in the rolling window as the new low-PE records displaced them one by one. Recovery to `trust_reasoning` began at approximately tick 40, once sufficient low-PE records had displaced the high-PE records. This 15-tick recovery lag after the shift to accurate prediction is architecturally correct: it reflects the biological observation that confidence in one's own reasoning recovers more slowly than it degrades, because a single correct prediction does not eliminate the evidence of prior miscalibration.

The Pack 4 100-tick simulation revealed that the policy bias accumulates substantially faster in the negative direction than in the positive direction at matched dopamine magnitudes. In the positive-reward phase (ticks 0-49, reward = 0.80, EMA starting at 0.50), the approach bias reached +0.022 by tick 49. In the negative-reward phase (ticks 50-74, reward = 0.20, EMA having risen to approximately 0.73), the approach bias fell to −0.117 by tick 74. The asymmetry arises from the relative magnitudes of the dopamine signals: in the positive phase, the EMA catches up to the reward within approximately 15 ticks, reducing subsequent dopamine signals toward zero and slowing further accumulation. In the negative phase, the EMA was elevated from the prior positive phase, so the negative dopamine signal remained large for the full 25 ticks before the EMA could adjust. This EMA inheritance effect is not an artefact; it is the correct biological behaviour. A system that has just experienced a sustained positive-reward phase and then encounters a negative-reward phase should exhibit stronger negative surprise -- and therefore stronger corrective bias decay -- than a naive system that has no prior expectation.

The Pack 1 verdict classification behaved exactly as predicted under the constant-input test conditions used in unit testing: constant inputs produce no observable trend, no slope magnitude reaches the stability threshold, and the verdict is `'inconclusive'`. This is not a failure of the classification logic. It is the correct epistemological response to a situation in which no evidence is available. The distinction between `'inconclusive'` under constant input and `'stable'` under genuinely improving input confirms that the observer layer discriminates correctly and cannot be fooled into a positive verdict by a stationary signal.

The 22-class waking chain produced by the completion of Day 30 exhibited clean sleeping-guard synchrony across all 16 new classes, consistent with the behaviour observed in Day 29. When `sleeping = True`, all Pack 1-4 Day 30 classes suppress their updates and produce no history entries. This synchrony ensures that the audit, regulation, calibration, and bias layers are all quiescent during sleep phases, preventing spurious statistics or bias accumulation from sleep-tick signal artefacts.

## VIII. System Limitations

Several architectural constraints in the Day 30 implementation are identified here and require documentation for the Day 31 forward plan.

The most significant current limitation is the absence of large-scale generalisation testing. All four Day 30 packs were validated under controlled synthetic simulations of 18 to 100 ticks. The longest single simulation run was the Pack 4 100-tick stabilisation test. These timescales are sufficient to validate the correctness of individual formulas, frozen constants, and boundary conditions, but they do not characterise the long-horizon behaviour of the 22-class chain under sustained real-organism operation. In particular, the interaction between Pack 3 confidence calibration and Pack 4 bias accumulation over thousands of ticks -- a scenario in which repeated calibration corrections continuously discount a bias that is simultaneously accumulating from consistent positive dopamine signals -- has not been characterised. Whether these two layers settle at a stable equilibrium or produce low-frequency oscillation in the adjusted confidence signal is an open empirical question that requires a long-horizon simulation to resolve.

The `SystemStabilityMonitor.audit_result` verdict is produced every waking tick but is not yet consumed by any upstream component. The verdict string is observable by any downstream component that reads from `systems['system_stability_monitor']` or `planning_sys.system_stability_monitor`, but no wiring currently connects a `'stable'` or `'unstable'` verdict to the sleep-onset probability, the cortisol signal, or the deliberation depth cap. This means the audit layer produces a correct measurement but exerts no architectural consequence. The completion of this feedback loop is among the highest-priority items for Day 31.

The Pack 2 `RecoveryStateManager` emits a `recovery_triggered` pulse and a sustained `recovery_active` flag, but no downstream component in the current architecture acts on these signals. Recovery is detected and classified but not acted upon. A recovery event could, in principle, reduce the adenosine level, reset branch weights toward initial values, or force a sleep-onset recommendation through the homeostatic drive system. None of these responses are wired. The `recovery_active` state is therefore currently a diagnostic signal rather than a control signal.

The interaction effects of the 22-class chain under adversarial or extreme conditions -- very high sustained prediction error, complete energy depletion, repeated suppression of all three action types simultaneously, or rapid oscillation between overload and healthy states -- have not been tested. The individual failure modes of each class are well-characterised, but the compound failure modes that emerge from interactions between multiple stressed subsystems are not.

## XI. Scientific Significance -- The Transition from Adaptive to Self-Correcting

Day 29 established that the organism is forced to be intelligent because the alternative is metabolically and survival-theoretically costly. Day 30 establishes that the organism is forced to remain intelligent under sustained operation -- that it cannot drift into pathological attractors, exhaust its own substrate, maintain overconfident commitments indefinitely, or fail to accumulate any preference structure over time.

These are not incremental additions. They represent the completion of a qualitatively different level of architectural organisation. A system that adapts but cannot verify its adaptation is a system whose performance claims are unfalsifiable from within. A system that adapts but cannot regulate its load is a system whose adaptation is fragile -- it operates correctly only under the metabolic conditions it happened to be designed for, and degrades catastrophically outside them. A system that adapts but cannot correct overconfidence is a system that will systematically misallocate deliberation to actions whose prediction quality does not warrant it. A system that adapts but forms no temporal preference structure is a system that cannot exhibit continuity of behaviour across repeated exposure to the same context.

Day 30 closes all four gaps within a single architectural session, under 106 validation checks distributed across the four mechanisms. The closing of these gaps is not merely a matter of adding more classes to the waking chain. It is a change in the epistemic status of the organism's adaptive behaviour: the organism now has grounds to claim that its loop is working (Pack 1), is sustainable (Pack 2), is calibrated (Pack 3), and is forming preferences in proportion to evidence (Pack 4). Each of these claims is not self-asserted by the organism; it is produced by an independent measurement layer that has been validated against known inputs under controlled conditions.

The broader architectural claim is that this quadruple regulation structure is what distinguishes a self-correcting organism from a self-modifying system. A self-modifying system changes its own parameters in response to feedback. A self-correcting organism monitors its own modification process, protects it from resource failure, corrects for the systematic biases in its own confidence estimates, and allows only bounded, evidence-proportional preference accumulation. Ikigai now satisfies all four criteria.

## XII. Day 31 Forward Direction

Day 31 will focus on adversarial validation, long-horizon simulation, and interaction analysis between the 22-class chain's subsystems under conditions not yet characterised.

The primary forward objective is long-horizon simulation. All Day 30 simulations ran for at most 100 ticks. The organism's 22-class chain has not been exercised for 5,000 or 50,000 ticks. At those timescales, several dynamics that were not observable in short simulations will emerge: the asymmetric branch weight recovery dynamics identified in Day 29 Pack 3, the interaction between sustained calibration correction and slow bias accumulation in Packs 3 and 4 respectively, and the behaviour of the stability audit verdict over periods long enough for the system to transition between `'inconclusive'` and `'stable'` multiple times. The target for Day 31 is a minimum 5,000-tick live-loop simulation with full telemetry across all 22 waking-chain classes.

The second objective is adversarial testing. The Pack 2 overload control has been validated under controlled strain scenarios but not under sustained adversarial conditions: rapid oscillation between overload and healthy states, simultaneous suppression of all three action types by the `HabitStabilityTracker`, and prediction error sequences designed to maximally stress the rolling window statistics of Pack 3. These conditions may expose interaction effects that are not visible in the nominal simulations.

The third objective is completing the feedback connections that Day 30 left open: wiring `SystemStabilityMonitor.audit_result` to upstream homeostatic parameters, and connecting `RecoveryStateManager.recovery_active` to the sleep-onset probability or the adenosine accumulation rate. Both of these connections would constitute genuine bidirectional coupling between the cognitive audit layer and the biological substrate, closing the loop between measurement and intervention that Day 30 left partially open.
