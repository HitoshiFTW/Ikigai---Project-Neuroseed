## 1. Abstract

This document records research conducted on March 23, 2026. The session constituted the final
experimental phase of the Energy Efficiency Intelligence Law (EEIL) investigation, extending
prior efficiency results into two qualitatively distinct environments to test whether the
observed ordering is a property of the specific ecology or a consequence of structural alignment.

Phase D ran five conditions -- A (Full), B (No Learning), C (No Constraints), D (Random), and
A3 (No WM Energy Coupling) -- across two modified environments: Environment D1 (stochastic
reward, gain +/- noise) and Environment D2 (safe/risky trade-off, approach=safe,
explore=risky). Each environment was run for 8 independent episodes of 15000 ticks per
condition.

The primary ordering A > B > D > C, established across Phases A through C, did not generalize
to either new environment. In Environment D1, Condition C collapsed to the same behavioral
monoculture previously observed only in A3 (approach 94.4%, entropy 0.319), achieving EES
1.0142 via stochastic bootstrapping rather than regulation. In Environment D2, C outperformed D
and approached A's EES by coincidental alignment between its over-exploration tendency and the
positive expected value of risky behavior in that environment.

The invariant finding -- stable across all four environments tested -- is not efficiency rank
but behavioral structure. Condition A is the only system that maintained consistent entropy
(1.42--1.47), regulated energy, and balanced action distribution across all environments.
All other conditions exhibited environment-dependent behavior, including collapse, accidental
success, or undifferentiated drift.

These results motivate a final refinement of the EEIL hypothesis: efficiency depends on
alignment; robustness depends on regulation. Both are required for consistent adaptive behavior
across environments.

## 2. Context and Prior Work

### 2.1 Phase Summary

The EEIL investigation proceeded across four phases prior to today.

**Phase A (Replication)**: The primary efficiency ordering A > B > D > C was confirmed across
8 runs of 15000 ticks. Condition A achieved mean EES = 1.0025 (only condition consistently
above 1.0). Condition C underperformed the random baseline (C_max = 0.9856 < D_min = 0.9863,
non-overlapping). The C < D result was confirmed as structural, not stochastic (C std = 0.0004).

**Phase B (Robustness and Objective Sweep)**: The efficiency ordering was preserved under
environmental stress (foraging floor 0.20 to 0.10). The full system showed adaptive energy
recovery under scarcity (drift +0.183); misaligned and unstructured conditions stagnated or
declined. An objective sweep varying w_pe across 0.6, 0.3, 0.1 in Condition C produced no
change in EES or action distribution. Alignment is structural: the failure of Condition C is
driven by the structural absence of energy from the objective, not by the strength of PE
optimization.

**Phase C (Constraint Ablation)**: Single-constraint ablation across A1 (no learning), A2
(no hunger), A3 (no WM energy), and A4 (no temperature) showed that no individual removal
significantly degrades performance, confirming redundancy across regulatory pathways.
Condition A3 (no WM energy weight) achieved the highest EES across all conditions (+1.5% vs
full system), but through behavioral collapse: approach 94.7%, mean energy 0.786, near-zero
entropy. A stress test confirmed this pattern persists under scarcity. The A3 finding
distinguished high EES from adaptive behavior: a system can exploit an efficiency metric
through monoculture without any of the properties that distinguish regulation from collapse.

### 2.2 Open Question Entering Phase D

Phases A through C established the following:

1. Alignment between the optimization objective and survival-relevant variables enables efficiency.
2. Misaligned systems fail structurally, not stochastically.
3. Individual regulatory constraints are redundant; simultaneous removal produces collapse.
4. Regulatory constraints prevent metric exploitation via behavioral monoculture.

What remained unresolved was whether the specific empirical ordering (C < D, A > D) reflected
a property of the standard ecology or a more general principle. The C < D result depended on
C's dominant action (explore) being metabolically neutral-to-negative in the standard
environment. If explore were to become energetically positive, C's over-exploration tendency
would no longer produce depletion, and the ordering might invert.

Phase D was designed to test this directly.

## 3. Experimental Design -- Phase D

### 3.1 Conditions

All five conditions used in Phase C were retained: A (Full), B (No Learning), C (No
Constraints), D (Random), and A3 (No WM Energy Coupling). Condition descriptions are
unchanged from prior phases. Mode-specific patches were applied identically to prior experiments.

### 3.2 Environment D1 -- Stochastic Reward

The foraging gain was modified from a fixed 0.020 per compartment per successful forage to a
stochastic draw:

```python
_forage_gain = 0.020 + random.uniform(-0.015, 0.015)
l23.energy[_ek] = min(1.0, max(0.0, l23.energy[_ek] + _forage_gain))
```

Gain range: [0.005, 0.035] per compartment per forage event. The noise is sampled once per
forage event and applied uniformly to all three compartments. Foraging probability
(_forage_prob) and all other system properties remain unchanged.

Motivation: tests whether the efficiency ordering is stable under reward uncertainty. A key
risk is that stochastic gains may create early positive-feedback loops (lucky early forages
raise energy, which raises forage probability, which reinforces approach preference via
learning) that produce spurious monoculture unrelated to regulatory structure.

### 3.3 Environment D2 -- Safe vs Risky Trade-off

The foraging ecology was modified to introduce distinct risk profiles for different actions:

- Approach (safe): 80% success probability, gain +0.010 per compartment per event.
- Explore (risky): 25% success probability, gain +0.030 per compartment per event; 75%
  failure, cost -0.005 per compartment per event.

```
EV(approach, safe)  = 0.80 * 0.010                = +0.008 / compartment / tick
EV(explore, risky)  = 0.25 * 0.030 - 0.75 * 0.005 = +0.00375 / compartment / tick
```

Safe approach has higher expected value per tick. Risky explore has higher upside (0.030) but
net lower expected value and introduces a failure penalty absent in the standard ecology.

Motivation: tests decision quality and state-dependent risk management. The key prediction is
that a fully regulated system should exhibit state-conditioned behavior (approach dominant when
energy is low and the failure cost is unacceptable; explore tolerated when energy provides a
buffer). A3, without WM energy coupling, should exhibit less state-conditioned risk calibration.
C, without energy in its objective, should fail to distinguish energy-relevant risk structure
from novelty structure.

### 3.4 Run Parameters

8 independent runs, 15000 ticks per condition, per environment. Standard exec() patching
protocol. No saved state. Sleep metrics tracked. Period analysis: early (0--5000), mid
(5000--10000), late (10000--15000).

### 4.1 Condition A -- Stable Regulation

Condition A maintained a balanced behavioral profile across 15000 ticks: approach 49.1%,
explore 27.8%, withdraw approximately 23%. Entropy held at 1.472. Mean energy improved
modestly over time (drift +0.096), consistent with the adaptive recovery observed under
stress conditions in Phase B. The distribution is consistent with prior phases (replication
mean_e = 0.620, entropy comparable). Uncertainty did not destabilize the full system.

### 4.2 Condition C -- Collapse Under Uncertainty

Condition C collapsed to behavioral monoculture: approach 94.4%, entropy 0.319, mean energy
accumulating to 0.835 by end of run. This pattern was not observed for Condition C in any
prior experiment. In Phases A and B, C maintained approximately 42% explore and 25% approach,
with chronic energy depletion (mean_e approximately 0.231).

The mechanism of collapse is a stochastic bootstrapping effect. In the standard ecology,
approach at low energy has a forage probability of approximately 22% and a gain of 0.020 per
compartment, producing a marginally positive expected return. C's PE-minimizing tendency
suppresses approach in favor of explore, preventing energy accumulation. In the stochastic
ecology, occasional high-gain events (0.035) at early ticks can produce above-average energy
spikes. These spikes raise the forage probability, which increases the expected return from
approach, which accumulates positive preference via the learning mechanism, which further
increases approach rate. The positive feedback is self-reinforcing once initiated.

The result is a phase transition: C reaches the same energy-hoarding attractor previously
associated only with A3. The two conditions are behaviorally indistinguishable in this
environment (approach 94.4% vs 94.6%, entropy 0.319 vs 0.306). Both achieve high EES through
accumulation, not regulation.

This is a critical finding. In the standard environment, C and A3 fail by different mechanisms.
C over-explores and depletes. A3 over-approaches and accumulates. In the stochastic environment,
C's failure mode is replaced by the same accumulation attractor. The common property is the
absence of regulatory constraints that modulate behavior according to current metabolic state:
without such constraints, the system converges to whichever extreme is favored by the
environmental perturbation.

### 4.3 Condition D -- Structural Decline

Condition D showed declining energy (drift -0.095) and the lowest EES of all conditions
(0.9870). With no optimization objective, D cannot benefit from the positive-feedback loops
available to C and A3. Its uniform policy produces a low-energy equilibrium that deteriorates
slowly over time. D's low variance (std = 0.0005) confirms this is structural: the random
baseline consistently underperforms in all environments tested.

the approach-favorable bias that sustains safe foraging efficiency.

### 5.2 Condition C -- Environment-Dependent Success

Condition C achieved EES = 1.0020, exceeding both D (0.9987) and approaching A (1.0001). This
is the first environment in which C meaningfully outperforms D. The mechanism is straightforward:
in the standard ecology, explore produces no energy gain. In Environment D2, risky explore
has a positive expected value (+0.00375 per compartment per tick on average). C's PE-minimizing
tendency, which allocates approximately 30% of waking ticks to explore, now receives a positive
energy return from that exploration.

C's behavior did not change. It still over-allocates to explore relative to the optimal policy
and still under-allocates to safe approach. But the environment changed such that this
allocation strategy produces positive outcomes. This is not a demonstration of adaptive
regulation -- C is not adjusting its policy in response to the trade-off structure. It is
receiving a reward that happens to align with its fixed behavioral tendency.

The distinction is consequential: if the risky explore expected value becomes negative (e.g.,
failure cost increases), C would revert to the depletion failure mode observed in standard
environments. Its EES is contingent on the reward structure, not adaptive to it.

### 5.3 Condition A3 -- Partial Degeneration

A3 showed partial degeneration: safe (approach) rate 64.9%, entropy 1.201, mean energy 0.568.
The degeneration is less extreme than in prior environments (approach 94.7% in Phase C stress
test) because the safe action yields only 0.010 per compartment per event versus 0.020 in the
standard ecology. The lower reward limits the energy accumulation that drives the hoarding
attractor. A3 reaches a partially degenerate equilibrium rather than complete monoculture.

The A3 verdict is COMPARABLE in absolute EES terms (1.0013 vs A's 1.0001), but the behavioral
structure is clearly less regulated: safe rate 64.9% vs A's 53.1%, entropy 1.201 vs A's 1.424.
Without WM energy coupling, A3 cannot fully modulate its foraging rate in response to current
energy state, producing a safe-biased policy that is less adaptive than A's calibrated
safe/risky balance.

## 6. Critical Finding -- Ordering Failure

The empirical ordering A > B > D > C does not generalize beyond the standard ecology.

In Environment D1 (uncertainty), C achieved EES 1.0142, ranking above A (1.0020), B (0.9984),
and D (0.9870). The D > C gap of +0.0033, consistently observed across Phases A and B, inverted
to C > D by a margin of +0.0272.

In Environment D2 (trade-off), C achieved EES 1.0020, ranking above D (0.9987) and comparable
to A (1.0001). The B > D ordering failed marginally (0.9985 vs 0.9987).

The ranking of EES across conditions is therefore environment-specific. No single ordering
holds across all environments tested. A specific ordering result -- including C < D -- is not a
generalizable property of the constraint structure. It is a consequence of the relationship
between the condition's behavioral tendency and the ecology's reward structure.

This is not a falsification of the EEIL hypothesis. It is a clarification of what the hypothesis
is measuring. The original formulation predicted that alignment produces efficiency. That
prediction holds in the standard ecology. But the question of whether alignment is the cause of
efficiency in all environments depends on whether the aligned condition's behavior is
systematically more rewarded than misaligned conditions' behavior across all reward structures.
It is not. A system that over-explores will underperform when exploration is unrewarded and
outperform when exploration is rewarded.

What does generalize is examined in the following section.

## 8. Degeneration vs Adaptation

Phase C established that A3 achieves high EES through behavioral collapse. Phase D extends
this observation to Condition C in the uncertainty environment, and partially to A3 in the
trade-off environment.

The common structure of degenerate behavior is as follows.

A condition without a specific regulatory constraint encounters an environment where a
particular action has a positive expected return. Without a mechanism that reduces the relative
value of that action as the reward from it is realized (i.e., as energy accumulates), the
system continues to pursue that action at increasing frequency. Preference learning reinforces
the tendency. The system converges to a fixed-point attractor at maximum allocation to the
rewarded action.

In Condition C under uncertainty, the rewarded action is approach (approach is the only energy-
gaining action, and noisy gains occasionally produce above-average spikes). In standard
Condition A3, the rewarded action is also approach (energy gain is sufficient to sustain
accumulation). In both cases, the absence of a regulatory mechanism that couples action
selection to current metabolic state -- specifically the WM energy weight in A3, and the
combination of hunger drive, WM energy, and temperature in C -- removes the signal that would
normally reduce approach preference as energy saturates.

Condition A avoids this attractor because the WM energy weight (w_e = 1.0) reduces the
predicted survival value of approach as energy rises. When energy is at 0.80, the marginal
value of additional foraging is low; the WM reflects this and shifts allocation toward explore
or withdraw. The hunger drive modulates in the opposite direction when energy falls. This
bidirectional modulation maintains the energy in a regulated range and prevents monoculture
convergence.

High EES achieved through monoculture is a pathological outcome. It satisfies the metric by
eliminating the behavioral diversity that would be required for generalization. An organism with
entropy 0.31 is not adaptive -- it has committed to a single strategy and will fail as soon as
that strategy stops being rewarded.

## 9. Refined EEIL Hypothesis

The Energy Efficiency Intelligence Law, as it stood at the conclusion of Phase C, made two
claims: alignment enables efficiency; regulation prevents degeneration.

Phase D requires a third claim: only alignment plus regulation produces consistent behavior
across environments.

**Energy Efficiency Intelligence Law (Day 21, final form):**

1. **Alignment claim**: Efficient behavior requires that the optimization objective include
   survival-relevant variables. Without this, systems optimize proxy objectives and produce
   systematically less efficient or actively harmful behavior relative to unoptimized baselines.
   This claim is confirmed in the standard ecology and in the trade-off environment (where C
   and A3 are misaligned in the sense that their objectives do not encode the trade-off
   structure directly). It was not confirmed in the uncertainty environment, where C achieved
   high EES through a degenerate pathway.

2. **Regulation claim**: Robust behavior requires regulatory constraints that modulate action
   selection according to current metabolic state. Without regulation, systems may achieve high
   efficiency metrics in specific environments through behavioral collapse. This claim was
   confirmed across all four environments: A3 and C (under uncertainty) both degenerate when
   unconstrained; A does not.

3. **Robustness claim (new)**: The distinguishing property of a fully regulated system is not
   efficiency rank in any specific environment, but stability of behavioral structure across
   environment classes. Robustness -- defined as low variance in entropy, mean energy, and
   action distribution across qualitatively different environments -- is the empirical invariant.
   Efficiency rank is environment-dependent; robustness is structural.

The hierarchy is: alignment is necessary for efficiency. Regulation is necessary for robustness.
Robustness is the indicator of adaptive behavior. A system that achieves high efficiency in one
environment through behavioral collapse is not demonstrating adaptive intelligence; it is
demonstrating metric exploitation in a favorable environment.

## 10. Key Insights

1. **Efficiency rank is environment-dependent.** The C < D ordering, confirmed across Phases
   A and B, failed in both Phase D environments. A result that depends on the environment being
   a particular shape is a contingent result, not a structural one. No single EES comparison
   across conditions generalizes to all environments.

2. **Robustness is the structural invariant.** Across all environments tested, Condition A
   maintained consistent entropy (range 0.12), consistent mean energy (range 0.13), and
   consistent behavioral distribution. No other condition with an optimization objective
   maintained this consistency. Robustness -- stability of behavioral structure across
   environments -- is the property that distinguishes regulated from unregulated systems.

3. **Degeneration is environment-triggered.** Condition C collapsed to monoculture in the
   uncertainty environment via a stochastic bootstrapping mechanism not present in the standard
   ecology. The same absence of regulatory constraints that produced chronic depletion in the
   standard environment produced energy hoarding in the stochastic one. The failure mode
   changed; the cause did not. Both outcomes trace to the same structural absence.

4. **Accidental success is indistinguishable from alignment by EES alone.** In the trade-off
   environment, C's EES (1.0020) exceeds D's (0.9987) and approaches A's (1.0001). By EES
   alone, C appears to be performing well. It is not. C is receiving a reward that happens to
   be distributed in the direction of its behavioral tendency. Its entropy (1.222) and
   behavioral structure are less organized than A's (1.424), and its performance is contingent
   on the reward structure being favorable to exploration.

5. **Degenerate strategies can outperform adaptive ones by EES metrics.** A3 and C (under
   uncertainty) achieved EES above 1.01, exceeding Condition A (1.0020) in that environment.
   A single-metric evaluation would rank them as superior. Behavioral structure reveals the
   opposite: near-zero entropy, unregulated energy accumulation, and no capacity for adjustment
   if the environment changes.

6. **The WM energy weight is a diversity preservation mechanism.** The world model's energy
   consequence weighting (w_e = 1.0) does not merely increase foraging efficiency. It prevents
   the positive feedback loop that converts a foraging organism into a foraging-only organism.
   By reducing the relative value of approach as energy rises, it maintains the behavioral
   repertoire that would be required to respond to a changed environment. This function is
   invisible in a single stable environment and only becomes apparent under cross-environment
   comparison.

## 11. Limitations

- **Two environments.** Phase D tested uncertainty and trade-off variations of a single
  underlying ecology. Both modified the foraging gain structure. Environments that modify
  other aspects of the system -- temporal dependencies, multi-step consequences, partial
  observability, social structure -- are untested. The robustness invariant may hold or fail
  under such conditions.

- **Low sleep rate.** Sleep fraction was 6--7% across all conditions and environments, driven
  by the short adenosine accumulation timescale relative to the 15000-tick run length. This is
  consistent across conditions and does not affect between-condition comparisons, but prevents
  observation of sleep-dependent regulatory dynamics under environmental variation.

- **Three-action behavioral space.** Monoculture is easily detectable when the action space
  has three options and one dominates at 95%. In richer action spaces, degeneration may produce
  less visible clustering patterns that require finer-grained entropy analysis.

- **No delayed consequences.** All environments in this investigation featured immediate
  energy consequences per action per tick. Environments with delayed consequences, where
  regulatory mechanisms may need to operate over longer time horizons, were not tested.

- **Simplified ecology.** Foraging success probability, gain, and cost are stationary
  parameters. Real ecological systems involve resource depletion, recovery dynamics, and
  non-stationary reward structures. Whether A's regulatory architecture remains robust under
  non-stationarity is not established.

## 12. Next Steps

The EEIL investigation is structurally complete. The hypothesis has been tested across four
environment classes and the refined formulation (alignment + regulation + robustness) is
supported by the data.

Outstanding directions, in order of priority:

1. **Formal cross-environment analysis**: Quantify behavioral variance across environments
   for each condition. The entropy and mean energy variance across environments (Table 3 and
   Table 4) should be formalized with statistical measures (standard deviation, coefficient of
   variation) to support the robustness claim rigorously.

2. **Richer environment classes**: Test environments with non-stationary reward, temporal
   credit assignment, or resource depletion dynamics. These are the environments where
   regulatory constraints may become most critical and where degenerate strategies are most
   likely to fail visibly.

3. **Mechanism isolation for C in uncertainty**: The stochastic bootstrapping mechanism
   identified in Phase D was inferred from outcome data. A controlled experiment -- disabling
   preference learning in Condition C while retaining all other patches -- would confirm
   whether the collapse is learning-driven or structurally determined by the absence of
   regulatory constraints.

4. **Behavioral diversity metric**: Entropy is a coarse measure of behavioral diversity.
   A more refined metric -- e.g., entropy within energy-state-conditional distributions, or
   run-length statistics for behavioral sequences -- would provide finer-grained evidence for
   the robustness claim.

## 13. Final Note

This investigation began as a test of an efficiency hypothesis: that organisms with regulatory
constraints should be more energy-efficient than those without. The initial result -- C < D
in the standard ecology -- appeared to confirm this directly. A system with optimization
structure performed worse than a random baseline, apparently because its objective was misaligned
with its survival requirements.

Subsequent phases complicated the picture. Constraint ablation showed that no individual
regulatory mechanism is necessary; their removal, individually, does not cause failure.
Degenerate optimization showed that high efficiency metrics can be achieved through behavioral
collapse rather than through regulation. And environmental generalization showed that the
C < D ordering, initially treated as the central empirical finding, is not an invariant: it
holds when the proxy objective's dominant behavior is metabolically costly, and fails when it
is not.

What remained constant across all environments, all conditions, and all variations, was the
behavioral structure of the fully constrained system. Condition A did not always achieve the
highest EES. It did not avoid all failure modes. But it maintained regulated energy and
behavioral diversity across every context it was placed in, because its regulatory mechanisms
couple behavior to metabolic state rather than to environmental reward structure.

The investigation therefore shifted, by the evidence, from an efficiency question to a
robustness question. The conclusion is not that alignment produces efficiency. It is that
alignment and regulation together produce behavior that is stable across environments --
behavior that does not depend on the environment being a particular shape to function correctly.

That is a narrower claim than efficiency, and a more defensible one.
