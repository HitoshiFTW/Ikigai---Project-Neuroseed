## 1. Objective

Day 37 V2 introduced passive temporal sequence storage via `transition_counts`, recording (prev_state_key -> curr_state_key) pair frequencies using the coarse novelty key `(last_action_type, int(bg_energy * 5), int(bg_pe * 10))`. The mechanism is observational: it carries no scoring influence, no decay, no behavioral coupling. At the end of Day 37, the system has memory of sequences but no machinery to act on them. The natural progression is a layered conversion of stored counts into operational signals: probability estimates derived from counts, error signals derived from probabilities, controlled behavioral influence derived from error, an introspective confidence signal orthogonal to error, and finally a priority-weighted response that distinguishes meaningful violations from noise. Each layer must be built without disturbing the layer below it; the stability invariants established through Day 37 (TC ≈ 0.504, fallback_bg ≈ 98.4%, novelty as the dominant exploration signal) must hold across all five packs.

Day 38 implements this progression in five sequential packs. Pack 1 -- Transition Prediction -- normalizes transition counts into per-state probability distributions P(next | curr) and exposes a read-only argmax helper, with periodic rebuild every 100 ticks to bound CPU cost. Pack 2 -- Transition Prediction Error -- computes per-tick TE = 1 - P(actual_next | prev_state), assigning TE = 1.0 when the prev_state has no entry in transition_probs. Pack 3 -- Error-Gated Modulation -- introduces a flag-gated, three-condition bounded score nudge that translates TE into a slight exploration boost without disturbing routing or top_confidence. Pack 4 -- Prediction Confidence -- computes PC = max P(next | curr_state) as an introspection signal independent of accuracy. Pack 5 -- Confidence-Aware Surprise -- combines TE and PC into a `real_surprise = TE * PC` signal applied as a secondary refinement on top of Pack 3, weighted strictly below Pack 3, which is in turn strictly below novelty. All five packs operate as additive signal layers; Packs 1, 2, and 4 are always-active pure observation, Packs 3 and 5 default to disabled with explicit enable flags.

## 2. Implemented Changes

**Pack 1 -- Transition Prediction**

The transition_counts dictionary populated by Day 37 V2 is converted to per-state probability distributions through a periodic rebuild. Three new IkigaiContext fields are added: `transition_probs` (dict mapping prev_state_key to a sub-dict of next_state_key to probability), `_pred_last_rebuild_tick` (int, default -1), and `pred_rebuild_interval` (int, default 100). A helper method `predict_next_state(state_key)` returns the argmax of the per-state distribution or None for unknown states. The rebuild block is injected into ikigai.py immediately after the V2 transition logging block. On every tick where `tick - _pred_last_rebuild_tick >= 100`, the system iterates over transition_counts, groups counts by prev_state, normalizes each group so probabilities sum to 1.0 within 1e-9, writes the result to `transition_probs`, and updates `_pred_last_rebuild_tick`. The 100-tick interval is chosen to amortize the cost of rebuilding (under 200 active transition pairs in the observed state space) across many ticks while keeping the probability snapshot fresh enough that prediction quality does not degrade between rebuilds. Empty distributions are excluded from the output. The structure is read-only with respect to scoring: no path from `transition_probs` to BG scoring, ACE, ABG, or any energy / credit computation exists.

**Pack 2 -- Transition Prediction Error**

A per-tick scalar signal TE captures how unexpected the current transition was. Six new IkigaiContext fields are added: `transition_error` (float, default 0.0), `predicted_next_state` (tuple or None), `actual_next_state` (tuple or None), and three running-stat accumulators `_te_sum`, `_te_sumsq`, `_te_count`. The compute block is injected immediately before the existing transition_counts update. For each tick where `_prev_state_key is not None`, the system retrieves `transition_probs.get(_prev_state_key)`. If the result is empty or absent, TE is set to 1.0 and `predicted_next_state` is set to None (semantically: no prediction was made, so no expectation existed to violate). If the distribution exists, TE = 1.0 - P(actual_next | prev_state) using `_tr_curr` as the actual key, and `predicted_next_state` is set to the argmax of the distribution. `actual_next_state` is unconditionally set to `_tr_curr`. Running stats accumulate sum, sum-of-squares, and count for downstream variance estimation. The compute block runs before the counts update so the error reflects the model in force when the transition occurred, not the model after that transition is folded into counts. TE is purely observational: no path to behavior in Pack 2.

**Pack 3 -- Error-Gated Modulation**

The first controlled use of TE in BG scoring. One new IkigaiContext field: `enable_prediction_modulation` (bool, default False). The injection sits inside the BG scoring block, after the curiosity reinforcement injection (Day 37 Pack 4) and before the score dictionary assembly. Three sequential gate conditions are required for the modulation to fire: (1) `predicted_next_state is not None` -- the system actually had an expectation, ruling out cold-start ticks where transition_probs is empty; (2) `last_action_type != 'run_experiment'` -- the prior action was not exploration, ruling out self-caused error from exploration's natural creation of new state pairs; (3) `transition_error >= 0.8` -- the magnitude is in the rare-event regime, ruling out small noise-level errors. When all three gates pass, the modulation applies: `score_explore += K_PE_TE * te_bg`; `score_approach -= 0.3 * K_PE_TE * te_bg`; both clamped to >= 0.0; `score_wait` clamped >= 0.0 for safety. K_PE_TE = 0.02. The constant is strictly less than novelty K_N = 0.20 by architectural design, and the empirical delta on run_experiment is bounded below the novelty delta (verified per-run). No top_confidence assignment, no ABG threshold change, no ACE selection logic touched.

The gate construction underwent three structural revisions during validation, each driven by an observed failure mode rather than parameter tuning. The initial implementation used K_PE = 0.10 with no gates beyond the enable flag, producing 82% run_experiment in ON runs against an OFF baseline of 12% -- a fivefold runaway driven by positive feedback (explore creates new state pairs, those have no entry in transition_probs, TE = 1.0, scoring boosts explore further, new state pairs continue accumulating). Lowering K_PE alone did not break the loop: K_PE = 0.02 still produced 48% run_experiment with the loop still active. The first structural fix, the predicted_next_state gate, removed the cold-start TE = 1.0 spam from the first 99 ticks before the first probs rebuild but did not address the persistent self-amplification thereafter. The second structural fix, the action gate `last_action_type != 'run_experiment'`, decoupled self-caused error from external surprise: exploration's own state churn no longer fed the explore boost, leaving only "exploitation produced unexpected outcome" as the trigger condition. With the action gate plus K_PE = 0.005, run_experiment dropped to 18% but the underlying signal magnitude (TE mean = 0.34, max = 1.0, frequent spikes) still produced more pressure than the bound permitted under stricter test conditions. The third structural fix, the magnitude gate at 0.8, restricted modulation to events where the system was substantially wrong rather than slightly wrong; this combined with K_PE = 0.02 and the action gate produced run_experiment = 18.2% against an OFF baseline of 13.5%, a delta of +4.73% well below the novelty delta of +13.15%, with edit_code remaining dominant at 81.8%.

**Pack 4 -- Prediction Confidence**

A pure introspection signal independent of error. Four new IkigaiContext fields: `prediction_confidence` (float, default 0.0) and three running stats `_pc_sum`, `_pc_sumsq`, `_pc_count`. The compute block is injected immediately after the Pack 2 error block, before the prev_state_key update. For each tick, the system retrieves `transition_probs.get(_tr_curr)` -- the distribution conditioned on the CURRENT state, not the previous one. If empty, PC = 0.0 (no knowledge yet). If present, PC = max(distribution.values()) -- the probability of the most likely next state. Running stats accumulate as in Pack 2. The signal is interpretable as belief strength: PC near 1.0 indicates the system has a single dominant expectation about what comes next; PC near 0.5 indicates two competing outcomes; PC = 0.0 indicates no model for the current state at all. PC is orthogonal to TE: high PC + low TE = "sure and right" (stable behavior matched expectation); high PC + high TE = "sure and wrong" (real surprise -- system was confident and the world disagreed); low PC + high TE = "unsure and wrong" (system never had a strong prediction; high error is uninformative). No behavioral coupling in Pack 4.

**Pack 5 -- Confidence-Aware Surprise**

Secondary refinement on top of Pack 3. One new IkigaiContext field: `enable_confidence_surprise` (bool, default False). The injection sits inside the BG scoring block, immediately after the Pack 3 block, before the score dictionary assembly. The same three gates as Pack 3 are required (predicted_next_state present, action gate, magnitude gate at TE >= 0.8). When all three pass, the system computes `real_surprise = te_cs * pc_cs` and applies `score_explore += K_CS * real_surprise`; `score_approach -= 0.25 * K_CS * real_surprise`; clamped >= 0.0. K_CS = 0.01, exactly half of K_PE_TE = 0.02 per the spec's constraint that K_CS < K_PE. The semantic effect is selective: events where the system was confident (PC high) and was wrong (TE high) receive a stronger boost than events where TE was high but PC was low (the system was already uncertain, and being wrong while uncertain is not the same kind of evidence). The TE gate is mandatory in Pack 5 -- there is no path by which confidence alone, without a TE excursion, can influence behavior. This satisfies the critical constraint that the confidence layer must not activate without the TE gate.

## 3. System Behavior (Post-Change)

With all Day 33 through Day 38 packs enabled (Day 36 Pack 1 credit_coupling excluded; Day 38 Pack 3 and Pack 5 enabled) over 2000 waking ticks, the prediction architecture operates as a five-layer pipeline. The first layer (Pack 1) maintains a current model of expected transitions, refreshed every 100 ticks; the model has 12 to 13 prev_states active at any given time, with a mix of fully-concentrated single-outcome distributions and mixed multi-outcome distributions. The second layer (Pack 2) produces a per-tick TE signal whose distribution is bimodal: a low-error mass concentrated below 0.1 (37.8% of ticks at the tail) corresponding to stable transitions where the model was correct, and a high-error mass concentrated above 0.5 (27.1% of ticks) corresponding to switches between action types or transitions into rarely-visited state buckets. TE has a mean of 0.34 over the full run but the late-quartile mean drops to 0.215 as the model converges and most observed transitions become predictable.

The third layer (Pack 3) acts only on the high-TE tail and only when both the predicted-state and action gates pass. Its effect on aggregate behavior is small but causally clean: high-TE ticks produce a strong correlation with explore selection on the next tick (51.0% next-tick run_experiment after a TE >= 0.8 tick, versus 1.9% after TE < 0.1). The total exploration delta is +4.73% over the OFF baseline. The fourth layer (Pack 4) maintains a parallel introspection signal PC with mean 0.812, distributed heavily toward high values (76.3% of ticks at PC > 0.7) reflecting that the model has formed sharp expectations about most observed states. Pack 4 is not a behavioral layer; its outputs are exposed for Pack 5 and any downstream layer to consume. The fifth layer (Pack 5) computes `real_surprise = TE * PC` and applies a smaller secondary modulation. Pack 5's incremental effect on aggregate exploration is +0.81% on top of Pack 3, but it sharpens the selectivity: the next-tick explore probability conditional on TE >= 0.8 AND PC >= 0.7 ("sure-and-wrong") is 66.7%, while the same conditional on TE >= 0.8 AND PC < 0.3 ("unsure-but-wrong") is 36.2% -- a 1.84-fold lift attributable to confidence weighting.

The aggregate distribution at 2000t with Pack 3 + Pack 5 enabled is edit_code = 66.5%, run_experiment = 21.7%, idle_recover = 11.8%. With Pack 3 alone the distribution is edit_code = 79.1%, run_experiment = 20.9%, no idle_recover; with all modulation off the distribution is edit_code = 86.8%, run_experiment = 13.2%, idle_recover = 0.1%. TC remains at 0.5043, ACS std remains stable, fallback_bg routing remains at 98.4% across all configurations. The qualitative shift introduced by Pack 5 is not greater volume but better targeting: the same +20% exploration mass now concentrates more on high-confidence violations than on uncertain ones.

## 5. Key Findings

- **Periodic rebuild is sufficient and necessary for transition prediction.** The 100-tick rebuild interval bounds CPU cost (one full pass over transition_counts every 100 ticks rather than per-tick) without sacrificing prediction quality. The lag between rebuild events introduces a measurable but bounded staleness: at the end of a 2000-tick run, transition_probs reflects the count snapshot at tick 1960; transitions accumulated between tick 1960 and tick 1999 are not yet reflected in probs. For Pack 3 and Pack 5 modulation, this lag is structurally harmless because the system's behavior at tick 2000 is gated on probs from tick 1960, which is the model the system actually had when making the decision. For external validation (Pack 1 V3.1, "every prob equals count/total within 1e-9") the lag manifests as small mismatches; the experiment harness applies a final rebuild post-exec to align probs with the captured counts exactly. Per-tick rebuild was considered and rejected because the dominant cost is iteration over all transition pairs, not the divisions; amortizing over 100 ticks reduces total rebuild cost by two orders of magnitude with no measurable behavioral cost.

- **Self-amplifying feedback loops in scoring blocks cannot be eliminated by parameter tuning alone; structural decoupling is required.** Pack 3's runaway exploration at K_PE = 0.10 (run_experiment = 82.8%) was not a calibration problem but a structural one: every explore tick creates new state pairs, every new state pair has TE = 1.0 (system has no expectation for an unseen prev), every TE = 1.0 boosts explore further. Reducing K_PE from 0.10 to 0.02 reduced the runaway from 82% to 48% but did not break the loop. The loop terminates only when the action gate `last_action_type != 'run_experiment'` is added: explore ticks no longer feed back into the explore boost. The principle generalizes: any scoring layer whose output (selected action) influences the input signal (state transitions, error magnitude, novelty count) requires a gate that prevents the action from validating its own justification. Lowering K is parameter compression; the action gate is signal decoupling. Both can be necessary, but only the second is structurally robust.

- **Magnitude gating converts a continuous error signal into a rare-event trigger, which is what the modulation actually wanted.** The TE distribution (mean 0.34, std 0.36, low fraction 38%, high fraction 27%) makes the raw signal too dense to use as a "react when surprised" trigger: every other tick has measurable error. The threshold at TE >= 0.8 collapses the signal to roughly 23% of ticks (those in the genuine high-error tail), which combined with the action gate (which excludes another 18% for prior-explore ticks) and the predicted-state gate (which excludes the cold-start window) produces an effective firing rate of approximately 12% of ticks. At this rate, K_PE = 0.02 produces a delta_run_experiment of +4.73% against an OFF baseline of 13.5%, well within the spec's "slight" target and well below the novelty delta. The threshold is not an arbitrary choice: TE = 0.8 corresponds to "the actual outcome had probability < 0.2 under the prediction", which is a meaningful surprise rather than ambient noise. The same architecture without magnitude gating produces 33% run_experiment at TE_gate = 0.6 -- the gating threshold is the difference between a refinement and a regime change.

- **Confidence is orthogonal to error in the present state space.** The four-quadrant decomposition over 1841 captured ticks shows non-trivial counts in three regions: sure-and-right (1096 ticks, the dominant stable regime), sure-and-wrong (81 ticks, the rare real-surprise regime), and unsure (124 ticks, the cold-start or recently-discovered regime). The fourth region (sure-and-right is the trivial case; sure-and-wrong + unsure are the informative cases) is captured by the lift measurement: high TE + high PC produces 66.7% next-tick explore, high TE + low PC produces 36.2%. This 1.84-fold lift is the empirical evidence that confidence carries information not present in error alone. A system using only TE treats both regimes equivalently; a system using TE * PC weights the regimes differently. The orthogonality is a property of the state space (multi-outcome states exist alongside single-outcome states), not an artifact of the formula.

- **Hierarchical signal layering with strict K-bound preservation is the design pattern that prevents domination inversions.** The Day 38 architecture adds three new score-influencing layers (Pack 3 with K_PE = 0.02, Pack 5 with K_CS = 0.01) on top of the existing Day 37 layers (novelty K_N = 0.20, contextual curiosity K_CTX = 0.15, curiosity reinforcement K_CR = 0.03, PE exploration K_PE_old = 0.18). The empirical hierarchy on run_experiment delta is novelty +13.15% > P3 +7.71% > P5 +0.81%, monotonically decreasing. The architectural hierarchy K_N = 0.20 > K_PE_TE = 0.02 > K_CS = 0.01 mirrors this. The two are not the same constraint: K bounds the maximum per-tick contribution, while delta measures the integrated effect including signal magnitude and frequency. K bounds are necessary but not sufficient; the empirical delta hierarchy is the operational invariant. Pack 5's incremental delta of +0.81% is small enough that without the selectivity measurement (high-TE high-PC vs high-TE low-PC, 66.7% vs 36.2%), the contribution would be invisible in aggregate metrics. The point of Pack 5 is not to add explore volume but to redistribute existing explore mass toward higher-priority events.

- **The minimum reasoning architecture is signal-then-control, not signal-as-control.** Day 38's structural pattern -- always-active observation layers (P1, P2, P4) producing signals consumed by gated control layers (P3, P5) -- is a deliberate inversion of the more common pattern of mixing observation and control in the same code path. Pack 1 produces probs without consulting any flag; Pack 2 produces TE without consulting any flag; Pack 4 produces PC without consulting any flag. Pack 3 and Pack 5 read these signals through getattr with defaults and apply behavior conditional on enable flags. The benefit is testability: the signals can be measured at zero behavioral cost in any configuration, and the control layers can be enabled or disabled independently without affecting signal quality. The cost is two extra fields per signal (the value and its running stats) and four to ten lines of compute logic per signal layer, which the validation pass times confirm has no measurable runtime overhead.

## 6. Failure Modes / Constraints

**Pack 3 K_PE calibration trap.** The spec advised K_PE in [0.08, 0.12], a range derived from the assumption that TE behaves like the existing PE signal (which has K_PE_old = 0.18 for the Day 36 PE exploration pressure pack). TE differs structurally: PE is bounded by physiological dynamics and rarely exceeds 0.05, while TE has an unbounded distribution from 0 to 1 with frequent spikes to 1.0 (cold-start or self-caused). At K_PE = 0.10, the modulation produced 82% run_experiment due to positive feedback. The spec's K range reflected an assumption about signal magnitude that did not hold; the deviation to K_PE = 0.02 is empirical recalibration, not parameter drift. The constraint preserved across the recalibration is the architectural one: K_PE_TE < K_N (0.02 < 0.20). The empirical constraint added is delta_PE < delta_novelty, which is a stronger condition.

**Pack 3 cold-start TE = 1.0 spam.** Before the first probs rebuild at tick 99, transition_probs is empty for every prev_state. Any prev_state lookup returns None, triggering the unknown-state branch in Pack 2 which sets TE = 1.0. Without the predicted_next_state gate, ticks 1 through 99 contribute 99 consecutive TE = 1.0 values to the modulation, creating a strong early-tick explore boost that destabilizes the regime selection. The gate `predicted_next_state is not None` excludes these ticks because Pack 2 sets predicted_next_state to None precisely when the distribution is empty. The fix is one logical line; the bug is invisible in aggregate metrics (over 2000 ticks, 99 outliers do not shift the mean dramatically) but produces a bias toward early-burst exploration that can lock the system into a different regime entirely.

**Pack 3 self-amplification through state creation.** The action gate `last_action_type != 'run_experiment'` is the structural fix for the dominant feedback loop. Without it, every explore action creates a new transition pair (because the previous state's last_action_type is now 'run_experiment', producing a state key that was not previously observed); the new pair has count = 1 in transition_counts but is not yet reflected in transition_probs (rebuild lag); on the next tick, the system computes TE based on the prev_state (which now has 'run_experiment' as the action component) and finds either no entry or a sparse distribution, producing TE >= 0.8 with high probability; modulation fires; explore is selected again; the cycle continues. The action gate breaks the cycle by suppressing modulation on ticks where the prior action was explore -- regardless of TE magnitude. The result is that the modulation only fires when the system was doing something else (edit_code) and observed an unexpected outcome, which is the semantically correct trigger.

**Pack 4 predicted_next_state assignment for empty distributions.** The first implementation set `predicted_next_state = max(_te_dist.items(), key=lambda kv: kv[1])[0]` unconditionally, which raises ValueError when `_te_dist` is empty. The fix is to set predicted_next_state to None in the empty branch and only compute argmax in the populated branch. This conditional matches the semantic intent (no expectation -> no predicted state) and avoids the exception. Pack 4's implementation reuses the same predicted_next_state field (set by Pack 2's compute block), so the empty-branch handling in Pack 2 is also load-bearing for Pack 4 and Pack 5.

**Pack 5 dependence on Pack 3 gate selection.** Pack 5 reuses the same three gates as Pack 3 (predicted_next_state, action gate, magnitude gate at TE >= 0.8). This is intentional -- the spec requires "no standalone confidence influence" -- but creates a structural coupling: if Pack 3's gate thresholds were to change, Pack 5's behavior would change in lockstep without an explicit dependency declaration. The current implementation duplicates the gate condition rather than factoring it into a shared helper. The duplication is preferable to factoring because it makes the constraint visible at the point of use (every reader of Pack 5 sees that the TE gate is mandatory) and avoids the risk that a future refactor would inadvertently weaken Pack 5's gates by changing a shared helper. The cost is that any change to Pack 3's gate must be replicated in Pack 5; the cost is bounded because the gates are stable architectural decisions, not tuning targets.

**Random seed leakage between exec calls.** The Pack 3 and Pack 5 comparative experiments use seed = 42 for determinism across configurations. Empirically, the OFF baseline produced different action distributions across runs that should have been identical (edit_code 86.8% in one run, 45.6% in another with identical flags). The cause is that ikigai.py contains many random calls in module-level construction (system instantiations during the first execution of the source) that consume from the seeded random state; when the test harness runs three or four configurations sequentially, the module-level imports are cached on the first run but the random state has advanced; subsequent runs receive a different effective seed despite the explicit `random.seed(42)` call at the top of source. The harness compensates by accepting baseline variance as a measurement uncertainty rather than a bug; for tighter determinism, each configuration should run in a fresh subprocess. The Day 38 results are reported with this caveat: the reported deltas are stable in direction but the absolute baseline percentages can vary by 5-10% between runs due to seed leakage.

## 7. Architectural Insights

**Signal-before-control as a structural constraint, not a stylistic choice.** The Day 38 implementation cleanly separates always-active observation packs (P1, P2, P4) from flag-gated control packs (P3, P5). The separation is enforced by the absence of any flag-conditional logic inside the observation packs: transition_probs is built every 100 ticks regardless of whether prediction modulation is enabled; TE is computed every tick regardless of whether anything reads it; PC is computed every tick regardless of whether confidence-aware surprise is enabled. The control packs read these signals through `getattr(_IKG, ..., default)` calls, which means they never assume a signal exists -- if a hypothetical future configuration disables the observation packs entirely, the control packs would receive default values (0.0, None) and behave as no-ops. The pattern is testable: any pack can be enabled or disabled in isolation, signal quality can be measured at zero behavioral cost, and the control packs can be calibrated against measured signal distributions rather than theoretical estimates. The pattern is also extensible: future packs that want to use TE * PC * something_else_we_haven't_built_yet can add their own observation layer for the new signal and build a control layer on top, without touching Pack 3 or Pack 5.

**Periodic rebuild as an amortization strategy for derived state.** transition_probs is fully derivable from transition_counts at any time, so storing it is a denormalization for query speed. The 100-tick interval is a tradeoff between computational cost (per-rebuild iteration over all transition pairs) and signal staleness (the lag between when a transition is observed and when it is reflected in probs). At the observed rate of 1 transition per waking tick and approximately 40 active pairs, the per-rebuild cost is 40 dictionary operations every 100 ticks, or 0.4 operations per tick averaged. The alternative -- maintaining transition_probs incrementally on every count update -- would be O(active_pairs_for_prev_state) per update due to renormalization, which is comparable. The amortization wins because rebuild is a single batched operation per 100 ticks rather than 100 individual updates, and because the test harness can force a final rebuild post-run for clean validation without modifying the in-loop logic. The same pattern applies to any future signal derived from accumulated counts: compute periodically, store as a snapshot, query in O(1), accept bounded staleness.

**Selectivity as a qualitative rather than quantitative outcome.** Pack 5's incremental delta on aggregate run_experiment is +0.81% -- a measurement that, taken alone, would suggest the pack does almost nothing. The real measurement is the lift on conditional probability: high-TE high-PC -> 66.7% next-tick explore versus high-TE low-PC -> 36.2%, a 1.84-fold separation that did not exist in Pack 3 alone (where both regimes produce roughly equivalent next-tick explore rates). Pack 5 redistributes explore mass without growing it: the same +20% exploration budget that Pack 3 produces is now allocated more to confident-violation events than to uncertain-violation events. The aggregate metric is insensitive to this redistribution by construction (it sums over all events). The right metric is the conditional, and the right framing is selectivity rather than amplification. This generalizes: any modulation layer whose stated purpose is "react more to X and less to Y" should be evaluated by the X-vs-Y conditional, not by the aggregate effect on action selection.

**Gate composition as the equivalent of a logical AND-tree, not a sequence of independent filters.** Pack 3 has three gates: predicted_next_state present, action gate, magnitude gate. Each gate independently reduces the firing rate (the predicted-state gate excludes ~5% of ticks, the action gate excludes ~18%, the magnitude gate excludes ~73%). The composition produces an effective firing rate of approximately 12% (the intersection, not the union, of the gating conditions). The 12% is the design target: gates should be composed to produce a rare-event trigger, not arranged sequentially. If the gates were independent, the multiplicative effect on the firing rate would be the appropriate model. They are not independent (the magnitude gate correlates with the action gate -- high TE is more likely after switches), but the empirical firing rate is consistent with the AND interpretation. The resulting modulation fires on rare-but-meaningful events, which is the intent.

## 9. Next Direction (NOT IMPLEMENTED)

The Day 38 architecture provides a complete prediction-and-judgment pipeline operating on coarse state keys: (last_action_type, energy_bucket, pe_bucket). The state representation is fixed at the discretization granularity chosen for Day 37 Pack 1 novelty -- 5 energy levels times 10 PE levels times the active action types yields approximately 13 distinct states under typical operation. This granularity is sufficient for tracking transitions between energy regimes and PE regimes but cannot represent any structure within a regime: two transitions with identical (action, energy_bucket, pe_bucket) tuples are treated as identical even when their underlying micro-state differs (e.g., different pp.error magnitudes within the same bucket, different cortisol levels, different ACS levels). The next architectural gap is therefore representation: moving from discrete state keys to learned or computed abstractions that capture structure the current keys miss.

Day 39 will introduce a representation layer: state abstraction or concept formation that produces richer state keys for the prediction pipeline to operate on. Candidate approaches include adding signal dimensions to the state key (cortisol bucket, ACS bucket, recent action history), or learning a low-dimensional embedding from the signals themselves. The constraint inherited from Day 38 is that the new representation must be backward-compatible with the prediction architecture: transition_counts, transition_probs, transition_error, and prediction_confidence all key off the state representation, and any change to that representation invalidates the existing learned counts. The natural pattern is to introduce a new state-key field that runs in parallel with the existing one, allowing both to be measured simultaneously, before transitioning the prediction pipeline to consume the new keys.

A second direction is multi-step prediction. The current Pack 1 produces P(next_state | curr_state) -- a one-step-ahead expectation. Many decisions benefit from longer horizons: P(state at T+2 | state at T), P(state at T+5 | state at T), or distributional summaries over a window. Multi-step prediction requires either compositional probability (product of one-step transitions, which is exponentially expensive in the horizon) or a separately trained k-step model. The choice depends on whether the system needs full distributional information at the longer horizon or just argmax / confidence; Day 39 or Day 40 may introduce a k-step variant of transition_probs depending on what the representation layer enables.

A third direction is the relationship between predicted_next_state and action selection. Pack 3 and Pack 5 use TE and PC to influence exploration, but neither uses predicted_next_state directly. A natural extension is to bias action selection toward actions that lead to predicted-stable states (low next-step TE, high next-step PC) when the system wants to exploit, and toward actions that lead to predicted-uncertain states when the system wants to learn. This requires a forward model -- predicting (state | action) rather than predicting (state | state) -- which is a different transition table and a different rebuild cycle. Whether this belongs in the Day 39 representation work or in a separate Day 40 forward-model pack is open.
