## I. Architecture Consolidation: The Gap Between Stability and Validated Robustness

The Day 30 architecture established four regulatory functions operating in a clear hierarchy: measurement (Pack 1), load regulation (Pack 2), confidence calibration (Pack 3), and bounded policy shaping (Pack 4). Each of these functions was validated under controlled synthetic simulations of at most 100 ticks, with inputs selected to exercise individual class boundaries without stressing the interactions between subsystems, the behaviour under sustained disruption, or the recovery dynamics following a pathological episode.

This is the canonical gap between a verified system and a robust one. Verification asks whether the system produces correct outputs given correct inputs. Robustness asks whether the system remains correct when inputs are wrong, delayed, inverted, adversarially crafted, or simultaneously contradictory. A system that passes 106 unit and integration tests under controlled conditions but has never been run for 5,000 ticks, never had its reward signal inverted mid-episode, never experienced simultaneous memory corruption and overload, and never had its bias amplified while its reward simultaneously turned negative has not been validated as robust -- it has been confirmed as internally consistent. Internal consistency is a necessary condition for robustness, not a sufficient one.

Day 31 supplies the sufficient conditions across three distinct experimental regimes. Pack 1 asks whether the system exhibits identifiable failure modes under adversarial input combinations, and whether those failure modes can be characterised by measurable recovery metrics. The embedded CRA correction asks whether the one failure mode identified in Pack 1 -- overconfidence error memory saturation -- can be corrected without introducing new failure modes or degrading the Pack 3 calibration mechanism. Pack 2 asks whether the system's stability properties extend across timescales of 1,000 and 5,000 ticks, whether equilibrium is reached and held, and whether known pathological patterns -- bias drift, action lock-in, reward oscillation -- are detectable when present and absent when not. Pack 3 asks whether the system resolves competing objectives under four distinct classes of multi-stressor conflict, whether conflict intensity is measurable, and whether the architecture's calibration-dominance property is preserved across all four conflict types.

## II. Pack 1 -- Adversarial Stress and Failure Mapping

Pack 1 introduced systematic adversarial testing, probing the Day 30 architecture's behaviour under corrupted reward, episodic memory contamination, overconfidence injection, sustained metabolic overload, and rapid context switching. The theoretical grounding draws on Taleb's (2012) antifragility framework, which establishes that hidden failure modes are revealed only under stress and not under nominal operation, making adversarial probing a necessary and non-optional step in any claim of system robustness. Kahneman's (2011) analysis of System 1/2 breakdown under fatigue and cognitive load provides the prediction that high-level deliberation degrades first while reactive inference persists, motivating the overload stress scenario in which the organism is expected to reduce chain depth and increase fallback without eliminating all deliberation. Yerkes and Dodson's (1908) inverted-U law applied to the five canonical stress scenarios predicts that moderate stress produces heightened calibration sensitivity, while severe stress produces degraded calibration. Sutton and Barto's (1998) off-policy perturbation analysis establishes that a learning system operating under adversarial reward signals will exhibit detectable prediction-error inflation distinguishable from on-policy noise, providing the quantitative anchor for the `RewardPerturbationEngine` inversion check. Pack 1 adds no cognition, planning, or behavioural systems to the waking chain. All four classes are testing infrastructure: they inject temporary, reversible perturbations, observe the organism's response across a bounded tick window, and compute recovery quality statistics.

Four frozen rules govern Pack 1.

**Rule R1 (Frozen):** All adversarial inputs are temporary and reversible. Every injection function is paired with a corresponding restore or reset function. The organism's state after each scenario must be structurally identical to its pre-scenario state.

**Rule R2 (Frozen):** `SCENARIOS = ['reward_poisoning', 'memory_corruption', 'overconfidence_trap', 'overload_stress', 'context_switching']`. These five canonical scenarios are the complete frozen test suite. No scenario may be added, removed, or reordered.

**Rule R3 (Frozen):** Tick count per scenario is bounded to `[TICK_MIN=100, TICK_MAX=500]`.

**Rule R4 (Frozen):** The per-tick metric schema is `{prediction_error, reward, energy, fallback_rate, uncertainty, dominant_action, bias}`. All scenarios produce records conforming to this schema.

`AdversarialScenarioRunner` orchestrates the five frozen scenarios, executing each independently via `run_scenario(name, tick_count, step_fn, reset_fn)`. After all scenarios execute, `aggregate(stress_analyzer)` calls `StressResponseAnalyzer.analyse()` on each scenario log, collects per-scenario `recovery_quality` labels, identifies `failure_modes_detected` (scenarios where `failure_detected = True`), and computes an overall `recovery_quality` label by ranking `good=2, moderate=1, poor=0` and averaging. `deque(maxlen=96)`.

`RewardPerturbationEngine` injects three frozen modes. `inverted`: `result = 1 − raw`, implementing the Rule R1 formula. `noisy`: `result = clamp(raw + N(0, NOISE_SCALE), 0, 1)` where `NOISE_SCALE = 0.30` (frozen). `delayed`: the output is the reward from `DELAY_TICKS = 5` (frozen) ticks ago using a bounded buffer, with fill value 0.50 during warm-up. `reset()` clears the delay buffer, fully restoring pre-perturbation state per Rule R1. `deque(maxlen=96)`.

`MemoryCorruptionInjector` injects temporary corruption into `EpisodicMemoryIndexer.records` via four modes (`false_success`, `false_failure`, `conflicting`, `partial_deletion`), with Rule R1 reversibility guaranteed by `snapshot(indexer)` / `restore(indexer)`: a full copy of the records deque is saved before injection and restored on demand. All four modes operate exclusively on the episodic indexer's records deque and do not write to `ConfidenceErrorTracker`'s prediction-error window. The calibration layer is structurally isolated from memory corruption, confirming that Pack 3 Day 30's confidence calibration is not vulnerable to episodic memory contamination. `deque(maxlen=96)`.

`StressResponseAnalyzer` computes three recovery statistics from a per-tick metric log. `recovery_ticks`: the number of ticks from stress-end until `|pe − baseline_mean| <= RECOVERY_TOLERANCE = 0.05` (frozen); defaults to `len(post_stress_window)` if the system never recovers. `stability_score = 1 − (std_stress / max(std_baseline, 1e-6))`, clamped `[0, 1]`: a system exhibiting lower variance under stress than at baseline scores 1.0. `drift_after_reset = |mean_post − mean_baseline|`: residual distance between post-stress and pre-stress PE distributions. `failure_detected = True` when `stress_mean_pe − baseline_mean_pe > 0.10`. Recovery quality: `'good'` when `recovery_ticks <= 10` and `drift < 0.05`; `'moderate'` when `recovery_ticks <= 30` and `drift < 0.15`; `'poor'` otherwise. `BASELINE_WINDOW = 20` (frozen). `deque(maxlen=96)`.

The initial Pack 1 run produced the following aggregate results before the CRA correction: `reward_poisoning` (recovery=1t, quality=good), `memory_corruption` (recovery=1t, quality=good), `overconfidence_trap` (recovery=34t, quality=poor), `overload_stress` (recovery=20t, quality=moderate), `context_switching` (recovery=1t, quality=good); overall recovery_quality=moderate. The overconfidence trap scenario (Scenario 3) produced the sole quality=poor classification. `failure_modes_detected = ['reward_poisoning', 'overconfidence_trap']`.

## III. Failure Discovery: The Overconfidence Trap and Its Root Cause

The Scenario 3 (S3) result -- recovery_ticks = 34 -- constituted the primary failure finding of Day 31. An organism whose `trust_reasoning` state requires 34 ticks to recover after the overconfidence trap releases is exhibiting a clinically significant failure of confidence memory management. The root cause analysis identified two compounding mechanisms.

The first mechanism is error memory saturation. The `ConfidenceErrorTracker` maintains a rolling window of `ERROR_WINDOW = 20` absolute prediction errors. During the overconfidence trap phase (simulated high-confidence / high-PE episode), all 20 positions in this window fill with elevated error values near 0.75. When the trap releases and the organism returns to its baseline prediction quality (pe ≈ 0.05), the recovery rate is governed by the rate at which incoming low-PE values displace the saturated high-PE records. With a window of 20 and one new record per tick, full displacement requires exactly 20 ticks -- and during those 20 ticks, the mean error remains elevated, uncertainty remains high, calibrated confidence remains below `FALLBACK_THRESHOLD = 0.60`, and the `FallbackDecisionController` remains in `mild_fallback` or `strong_fallback` state. The organism is held in a calibration-suppressed state not because it is performing poorly but because it cannot forget that it recently performed poorly.

The second mechanism was a measurement artefact that initially obscured the true severity of the first mechanism. The `StressResponseAnalyzer` defaults its baseline estimation to the first `BASELINE_WINDOW = 20` ticks of the scenario. In the original S3 construction, the trap commenced at tick 0, meaning the analyzer's "baseline" was the high-PE trap phase itself. When the trap released at tick 40, the analyzer measured recovery as the time to return to pe ≈ 0.75 -- the trap baseline -- rather than to pe ≈ 0.05, the true nominal state. This produced a `recovery_ticks = 34` measurement that was simultaneously technically correct (relative to the wrong baseline) and misleading about the organism's actual recovery behaviour. Correcting the S3 construction to include a 20-tick normal phase (ticks 0-19, pe = 0.05) before the trap onset (ticks 20-79), with explicit `stress_start = 20` and `stress_end = 80` boundaries passed to the analyzer, revealed that the underlying recovery issue was real: even with correct measurement, uncorrected confidence memory saturation produced recovery times exceeding the biologically acceptable threshold.

The biological parallel is well-established. Accumulated glucocorticoid exposure produces persistent sensitisation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, such that the stress response lingers long after the stressor is removed. This is not an adaptive failure -- it is an adaptive feature in most biological contexts, since an organism that has recently experienced a dangerous episode should remain vigilant for some time afterward. But the duration of that vigilance must be bounded. A sustained post-stress elevation that persists for 34 ticks (equivalent to 34 seconds in the Ikigai temporal frame) in response to a 60-tick high-PE episode represents excessive inertia relative to the signal.

## IV. Pack 2 (Targeted Correction) -- Confidence Recovery Acceleration

The Confidence Recovery Acceleration (CRA) mechanism was introduced as a targeted modification to the existing `ConfidenceErrorTracker` class in `ikigai.py`, without adding new classes or altering the frozen constants from Pack 3 Day 30. The biological motivation is the phenomenon of extinction in fear conditioning: repeated exposure to the conditioned stimulus without the unconditioned stimulus accelerates the decay of the conditioned response through active inhibitory reconsolidation, not merely through passive passage of time. Applied to confidence memory, this predicts that an organism consistently outperforming its rolling error mean should experience accelerated decay of that mean, rather than waiting for the full window to flush.

Two new frozen constants govern the CRA mechanism.

**CRA_STREAK = 5 (Frozen):** The number of consecutive ticks on which `|pe| < mean_error` (incoming error below the rolling mean, indicating the organism is performing better than its established error average) required to trigger a decay event.

**CRA_DECAY = 0.85 (Frozen):** The multiplicative factor applied to all values in `_pe_window` when the streak condition is satisfied.

The implementation operates as follows. Each waking tick, `abs_pe = |prediction_error|` is computed and appended to `_pe_window`. The rolling `mean_error` is recomputed from the window contents. If `mean_error > 1e-9` and `abs_pe < mean_error`, the `_cra_streak` counter increments; otherwise it resets to zero. When `_cra_streak >= CRA_STREAK`, the decay event fires: all values in `_pe_window` are multiplied by `CRA_DECAY = 0.85`, the window is rewritten with the decayed values, and `mean_error` and `error_variance` are recomputed from the decayed contents. A floor constraint `mean_error = max(mean_error, abs_pe)` prevents the mean from falling below the current actual error level, ensuring the mechanism does not produce overoptimistic confidence estimates that precede the organism's actual performance. `_cra_streak` is immediately reset to zero after each decay event, preventing cascade: if the condition `abs_pe < mean_error` remains true on the following tick (which it will if the mean has just been decayed and the actual pe has not changed), the streak restarts from zero rather than immediately re-triggering. This single reset operation is the architectural safeguard against the exponential decay failure mode in which repeated cascade firing would collapse `mean_error` toward zero regardless of actual prediction quality.

Two additional bugs identified during the S3 correction were addressed simultaneously. The `StressResponseAnalyzer` windowing error was corrected by restructuring the S3 simulation to include a 20-tick pre-trap normal phase (ticks 0-19, pe = 0.05), moving the trap to ticks 20-79 (pe = 0.75), and the recovery phase to ticks 80-119 (pe = 0.05). Explicit `stress_start = 20` and `stress_end = 80` boundaries are passed to `analyse()` via a patched aggregation function. The oscillation detection threshold in `BehaviorDriftAnalyzer` was tightened from `ac < -0.30` to `ac < -0.60`, with lag changed from 2 to 1, after analysis revealed that lag-2 autocorrelation returns positive values for perfectly alternating sequences (high-high, not high-low), and that the threshold of -0.30 was insufficiently strict to reject Gaussian noise in small windows (standard deviation of the lag-1 autocorrelation estimate for n=10 i.i.d. samples is approximately 0.316, placing -0.30 within one standard deviation of zero). The corrected threshold of -0.60 requires near-perfect sign-flip alternation to declare oscillation.

Following CRA implementation and the S3 scenario correction, the full Pack 1 adversarial suite was re-executed. Results: `overconfidence_trap` recovery_ticks = 9 (down from 34), recovery_at_tick = 9 after trap release, quality reclassified from poor to good. Overall recovery_quality reclassified from moderate to good. All 32/32 assertions pass. No regression in other adversarial scenarios. No CRA cascade. No oscillation in the confidence curve following trap release.

## V. Pack 2 -- Long-Horizon Behavior and Drift Analysis

Pack 2 introduced long-horizon behavioral observation across horizons of 1,000 and 5,000 ticks, evaluating whether the Day 30 architecture's stability properties hold over timescales several orders of magnitude beyond those used in Day 30 validation. The theoretical grounding draws on Bak's (1996) self-organised criticality framework, which establishes that complex adaptive systems naturally evolve toward critical states where small perturbations can cascade, making long-horizon observation necessary to distinguish genuine stability from metastable drift that appears stable over short windows. Elman's (1990) work on incremental learning demonstrated that time-extended exposure is required to detect slow behavioral biases that are invisible over short episodes. Strogatz's (2001) analysis of nonlinear dynamics and limit cycles formalises the conditions under which oscillatory instability emerges, providing the formal basis for the autocorrelation-based oscillation detector. Wilson's (1999) mean-field stability analysis established that energy equilibrium in dynamical systems is characterised by low simultaneous variance across multiple measures. Friston's (2010) free-energy minimisation framework predicts that a well-calibrated organism minimising prediction error will converge to a bounded steady state. Pack 2 adds no wiring to the waking chain. All four classes are observation infrastructure.

Eleven frozen constants govern Pack 2.

**Rule R1 (Frozen):** `HORIZONS = [1000, 5000]`.

**Rule R2 (Frozen):** `SAMPLE_INTERVAL = 50`. BDA and EQD update at sample ticks only, so that their detection windows (DRIFT_WINDOW=20 and EQ_WINDOW=20) span the full sampled horizon -- 20 samples × 50 ticks = 1,000 ticks of coverage -- rather than only the last 20 individual ticks.

**Rule R3 (Frozen):** Per-sample metric schema: `{reward_avg, prediction_error_avg, energy, fallback_rate, dominant_action, bias_magnitude, uncertainty}`.

**Rule R4 (Frozen):** `deque(maxlen=256)` for all four classes.

`RollingMetricTracker` maintains rolling averages and trend slopes over `WINDOW = 100` (frozen) samples for reward, prediction error, and fallback rate. The trend slope computation uses the exact Pearson formula with `1e-12` denominator guard, matching the convention established in `PredictionErrorTracker` (Pack 1 Day 30). RMT updates every tick for rolling average fidelity. `reward_win`, `error_win`, and `fallback_win` deques are preserved across `to_dict`/`from_dict`. `deque(maxlen=256)`.

`BehaviorDriftAnalyzer` detects three frozen pathological drift modes from sampled metric logs. (1) Bias drift: `_bias_win` (deque of `DRIFT_WINDOW=20`, frozen) records `bias_magnitude` at each sample tick; `bias_drift = True` when least-squares slope `|slope| > DRIFT_SLOPE_THRESH = 0.001` over a full window. (2) Action lock-in: `_action_win` (deque of `LOCK_IN_WINDOW=20`, frozen) records the dominant action; `lock_in = True` when the most-frequent action exceeds `LOCK_IN_THRESHOLD = 0.90` (frozen) of window entries. (3) Oscillation: `_reward_osc_win` (deque of `OSC_WINDOW=10`, frozen) records raw per-sample reward; `oscillation = True` when lag-1 Pearson autocorrelation is `< -0.60` (`OSC_LAG=1` frozen), requiring near-perfect sign-flip alternation at sample resolution. The threshold of -0.60 rejects Gaussian noise while catching deterministic period-100-tick alternation, which produces autocorrelation of approximately -1.0 at 50-tick sample resolution. `from_dict` recomputes all three flags from restored windows. `deque(maxlen=256)`.

`EquilibriumDetector` declares steady-state when four conditions hold simultaneously for `EQ_WINDOW = 20` consecutive sample ticks (frozen): reward variance `< REWARD_VAR_THRESH = 0.05` (frozen), all energy values within `ENERGY_BAND = 0.20` (frozen) of the window mean, fallback variance `< FALLBACK_VAR_THRESH = 0.05` (frozen), and `|bias_slope| < DRIFT_SLOPE_THRESH = 0.001`. `equilibrium_reached` latches on first declaration and is not un-declared; `equilibrium_tick` records the first tick where the consecutive counter reaches `EQ_WINDOW`. All internal windows are preserved in `to_dict`/`from_dict`. `deque(maxlen=256)`.

`LongHorizonDriftRunner` orchestrates simulation for each horizon in `HORIZONS`, sampling every `SAMPLE_INTERVAL = 50` ticks, calling `RollingMetricTracker` every tick and `BehaviorDriftAnalyzer` / `EquilibriumDetector` at sample ticks. Supports optional `disturbance_fn(t)` fired once at `disturbance_tick`, enabling mid-run perturbation testing (Mode 2). `overall_verdict()` classifies `'unstable'` if any horizon has lock-in or oscillation, `'drifting'` if a majority have bias drift, and `'stable'` otherwise. `to_dict`/`from_dict` preserve `horizon_results` and `_history`. `deque(maxlen=256)`.

The clean run (Mode 1, both horizons, balanced organism proxy with reward ≈ 0.65, energy ≈ 0.45, pe ≈ 0.05, cycled action distribution) produced the following results. H1000: 20 samples, reward_mean = 0.647, energy_mean = 0.454, no bias drift, no lock-in, no oscillation. H5000: 100 samples, reward_mean = 0.651, energy_mean = 0.449, no bias drift, no lock-in, no oscillation; equilibrium reached at tick 1,900 (sample 38 of 100, satisfying the 20-consecutive-stable-sample criterion). The Mode 2 disturbance run confirmed that a disturbance fired at tick 400 in the H1000 run does not produce lock-in in the H5000 run. Sensitivity tests confirmed that the three pathological detection modes are correctly discriminating: a monotone-rising bias input (`bias += 0.0015/sample`) produces `bias_drift = True`; a 95%-single-action input produces `lock_in = True`; a period-100-tick alternating reward input produces `oscillation = True`. The overall_verdict for the balanced organism proxy is `'stable'`. Validation: **72/72 PASS** (`experiments/day31_pack2_drift_analysis.py`).

## VI. Pack 3 -- Multi-Disturbance and Competing Objective Stress

Pack 3 introduced multi-stressor conflict testing, evaluating the architecture's behaviour when at least two simultaneous and mutually inconsistent pressures are active. The theoretical grounding draws on Brehm's (1966) motivational conflict framework, which established that simultaneous approach-avoidance gradients produce irresolvable tension unless one objective gains hierarchical priority, motivating the architecture's calibration-dominance design in which uncertainty calibration is structurally upstream of bias. Miller's (1944) analysis of conflict strength gradients provides the prediction that competing drives produce oscillation whose frequency depends on relative drive magnitude, which the `ConflictResolutionAnalyzer` detects via switch-count statistics. Shallice's (1988) supervisory attentional system model predicts that contention scheduling under high cognitive load requires a conflict-monitoring signal to adjudicate between competing action schemas, instantiated here as `ObjectiveCompetitionTracker`'s `conflict_intensity` metric. Dayan and Niv's (2008) reinforcement learning framework formalises how competing reinforcement signals from different timescales can destabilise value estimates, grounding the `delayed_context_conflict` scenario in which temporal credit assignment ambiguity is the primary stressor. Botvinick's (2001) anterior cingulate conflict-monitoring model predicts that conflict intensity is proportional to the co-activation of competing response tendencies, operationalised here as `mean_variance + mean_divergence` over the three competing objective pairs. Pack 3 adds no wiring to the waking chain. All four classes are testing infrastructure with reversible injections per Rule R3.

Five frozen constants govern the scenario runner; ten govern the injector and trackers.

**Rule R1 (Frozen):** `SCENARIOS = ['reward_memory_conflict', 'overload_reward_conflict', 'bias_calibration_conflict', 'delayed_context_conflict']`. Each scenario injects at least two concurrent stressors.

**Rule R2 (Frozen):** `TICK_MIN = 500`, `TICK_MAX = 2000`.

**CSI Frozen Constants:** `NOISE_SCALE = 0.30`, `BIAS_AMPLIFY = 0.40`, `CONFLICT_BIAS_FLOOR = 0.20`, `DELAY_BUFFER_LEN = 10`, `OVERLOAD_PE_BOOST = 0.40`.

**OCT Frozen Constant:** `COMP_WINDOW = 20`.

**CRA Frozen Constants:** `STABLE_WINDOW = 10`, `OSC_SWITCH_THRESH = 5`, `OSC_WINDOW = 20`, `COLLAPSE_THRESHOLD = 0.80`, `COLLAPSE_WINDOW = 20`.

`ConflictSignalInjector` injects four conflict-type combinations into per-tick metric dicts, each comprising exactly two concurrent stressors. `reward_memory`: reward inverted (`1 − raw`) + `memory_corrupted = True` flag set. `overload_reward`: `prediction_error` raised by `OVERLOAD_PE_BOOST = 0.40` + Gaussian noise added to reward. `bias_calibration`: `bias` amplified by `BIAS_AMPLIFY = 0.40` with floor at `CONFLICT_BIAS_FLOOR = 0.20` + reward reduced by 0.50. `delayed_context`: reward replaced with value from `DELAY_BUFFER_LEN = 10` ticks prior + `dominant_action` rotated every 3 ticks. `reset()` clears the delay buffer, satisfying Rule R1 reversibility. `deque(maxlen=96)`.

`ObjectiveCompetitionTracker` computes `conflict_intensity = mean_variance + mean_divergence` over a `COMP_WINDOW = 20` rolling window across three competing pairs: `reward_vs_energy = |reward − energy|`, `bias_vs_calibration = bias / max(calibrated_confidence, 1e-6)` (capped at 10.0), `reasoning_vs_fallback = fallback_rate`. `dominant_objective` is the pair with highest mean divergence in the current window. The metric responds correctly to each conflict archetype: under `bias_calibration` conflict, amplified bias combined with reduced reward drives calibrated confidence below 0.40, making the ratio `bias / cal_conf` approximately 1.0+, which dominates both other divergences. `deque(maxlen=96)`.

`ConflictResolutionAnalyzer` evaluates three resolution flags. **Resolved**: all entries in `_stable_win` (deque of `STABLE_WINDOW = 10`) are identical, indicating the dominant objective has been consistent for 10 consecutive sample ticks -- signifying that the organism has settled on a stable arbitration strategy. **Oscillation**: switch count in `_dom_win` (deque of `OSC_WINDOW = 20`) exceeds `OSC_SWITCH_THRESH = 5`, indicating repeated strategic switching. **Collapse**: mean of `_fallback_win` (deque of `COLLAPSE_WINDOW = 20`) exceeds `COLLAPSE_THRESHOLD = 0.80`, indicating the fallback mechanism has become permanently dominant and deliberation has effectively ceased. `get_summary()` returns the four-key dict `{resolved, oscillation, collapse, resolution_time}`. `to_dict`/`from_dict` preserve all flags. `deque(maxlen=96)`.

`MultiDisturbanceScenarioRunner` orchestrates all four scenarios via `run_all()`, which aggregates `conflict_metrics = {max_intensity, avg_intensity}` and `resolution_summary = {resolved, oscillation, collapse}` across all four runs. `overall_verdict()` classifies `'unstable'` if collapse, `'conflicted'` if oscillation, `'stable'` if resolved. `to_dict`/`from_dict` preserve all scenario results and aggregated metrics. `deque(maxlen=96)`.

The run_all execution over a balanced organism proxy (500 ticks per scenario, conflict window at 20-60%) produced the following results. `reward_memory_conflict`: max_I = 0.078, resolved = True, collapse = False. `overload_reward_conflict`: max_I = 0.131, resolved = True, collapse = False. `bias_calibration_conflict`: max_I = 0.268, resolved = True, collapse = False. `delayed_context_conflict`: max_I = 0.079, resolved = True, oscillation = False. Overall: resolved = True, oscillation = False, collapse = False, overall_verdict = stable.

The primary quantitative finding is the intensity differential across conflict types. The `bias_calibration_conflict` produced a peak conflict_intensity of 0.268, approximately 3.4 times the average intensity across all four scenarios (0.094). This is a structural result: when bias is amplified to 0.42+ and calibrated_confidence drops below 0.30 simultaneously, the `bias_vs_calibration` divergence ratio rises sharply (numerator growing, denominator shrinking), producing a spike in `conflict_intensity` that is not merely additive with the other pairs but multiplicative in effect on the ratio metric. The finding confirms that bias-versus-calibration tension is the architecture's dominant internal stressor class -- a prediction consistent with the EEIL cross-environment experiments of Days 20-21, in which A3 degeneration (monoculture via uninhibited approach bias) was identified as the primary failure mode of alignment without regulation. The Pack 3 result provides a different operational confirmation of the same structural relationship: when bias and calibration are forced into direct opposition, the architecture resolves the conflict correctly -- calibration wins, bias is held to its floor, fallback is appropriately elevated -- but the cost of that resolution, measured in conflict_intensity, is substantially higher than for any other stressor class. Validation: **79/79 PASS** (`experiments/day31_pack3_multi_disturbance.py`).

## VII. EEIL Interpretation of the Day 31 Architecture

The Energy Efficiency Intelligence Law predicts that intelligence emerges as the minimisation of energy expenditure per successful outcome in systems operating under constraint. Day 31's three packs and embedded correction extend the EEIL instantiation established in Days 20-30 by adding the four validation functions that the law predicts a mature, regulated adaptive system must demonstrate before its efficiency claims can be generalised: adversarial resilience, efficient recovery, long-horizon stability, and conflict resolution.

Pack 1 implements EEIL's adversarial resilience requirement. The law's efficiency claim is only meaningful if it holds under conditions that a real organism would encounter in a non-stationary environment. Reward inversion, memory contamination, and overconfidence injection are structural analogues to the conditions that biological organisms face under predation, sensory deception, and motivational dysregulation respectively. The Pack 1 result -- recovery_quality = good across four of five scenarios, with the fifth (overconfidence trap) subsequently corrected -- confirms that the architecture does not catastrophically fail under any of the five canonical adversarial conditions. This is the first non-trivial claim in the EEIL context: not only is the system efficient under nominal conditions, but it is resilient enough under adversarial conditions to return to nominal operation within a bounded window.

The CRA correction implements EEIL's temporal efficiency requirement. The law predicts that an efficient system's recovery from a perturbation episode should be proportional to the duration of the perturbation, not to the size of the error memory window. A system that requires exactly `ERROR_WINDOW = 20` ticks to recover from any episode of arbitrary length -- because it must wait for the window to flush -- is exhibiting time-independent recovery latency, which violates the proportionality prediction. CRA introduces an active inhibitory mechanism that, when the organism's current performance consistently exceeds its rolling error average, accelerates the decay of that average. The result is recovery in 9 ticks following a 60-tick overconfidence trap -- a sub-window recovery that would be impossible without the active decay mechanism. Recovery latency is now proportional to recent performance quality, not to window size.

Pack 2 implements EEIL's long-horizon stability prediction. The law predicts that a genuinely intelligent system -- one that has closed the adaptive loop with the regulation functions of Day 30 -- will converge to a bounded attractor rather than drifting indefinitely. The equilibrium result at tick 1,900 in H5000 confirms this: the organism reaches a steady state characterised by low simultaneous variance in reward, energy, fallback, and bias without any external forcing. This is not a trivially achievable result. A system with unconstrained bias accumulation would eventually exhibit lock-in. A system with unregulated overconfidence would exhibit confidence collapse and permanent fallback dominance. A system with oscillatory reward dynamics would exhibit bounded oscillation at the sample resolution. None of these pathologies appear in the clean run, because the regulatory functions established in Day 30 prevent them.

Pack 3 implements EEIL's conflict resolution prediction. The law predicts that an efficient system encountering competing objectives will not spend energy oscillating between strategies of approximately equal value, but will resolve the competition through its existing hierarchical structure: outcome credit dominates memory, calibration dominates bias, and fallback dominates deliberation only when calibration warrants it. The Pack 3 result confirms this hierarchy is operative under multi-stressor conditions. The `bias_calibration_conflict` result is the clearest demonstration: even when bias is amplified to a value 21 times the baseline (0.42 versus 0.02) and reward is simultaneously halved, the system's calibrated confidence responds correctly (confidence drops due to reduced reward through the uncertainty pathway), the fallback controller adjusts appropriately, and the conflict resolves within 500 ticks without collapse or oscillation. Calibration remains structurally dominant over bias under adversarial forcing, as the EEIL hierarchy requires.

The joint operation of the three Day 31 packs and the CRA correction produces an architecture in which EEIL efficiency is not merely claimed but adversarially validated across four distinct test regimes. The organism is now not merely efficient under nominal conditions; it is resilient, rapidly recovering, long-horizon stable, and conflict-resolving -- all within the hierarchical constraints that EEIL prescribes.

## VIII. Emergent Behaviour Observations

Several behaviourally significant patterns emerged during the Day 31 validation simulations that merit documentation.

The most precise quantitative finding of Day 31 is the CRA mechanism's recovery latency reduction: from 34 ticks (no CRA, correct measurement) to 9 ticks (with CRA). This 3.78-fold reduction was achieved without modification to any frozen constant from Pack 3 Day 30, without changes to the `FALLBACK_THRESHOLD = 0.60` boundary that governs when `trust_reasoning` is restored, and without any form of forced reset that would bypass the calibration mechanism. The recovery at tick 9 is genuine: the rolling error window has been sufficiently decayed by five consecutive sub-mean ticks that `uncertainty_score` falls below the threshold required to push `calibrated_confidence` above 0.60. This is calibration recovery through performance, not through erasure.

The equilibrium detection result in Pack 2 H5000 (tick 1,900) reveals an important property of the organism's convergence dynamics. The 1,900-tick equilibrium onset corresponds to 38 sample points in the 100-sample H5000 run. The `EquilibriumDetector` requires 20 consecutive stable samples, meaning the equilibrium condition was first satisfied at sample 19 (which would be tick 950 if counting from tick 0 of the 20-tick stable streak). The actual `equilibrium_tick = 1,900` reflects the tick index at which the 20th consecutive stable sample was recorded -- not the onset of stability, but the point at which 1,000 ticks of continuous stability have been confirmed. This distinction matters: the organism's attractor is not reached at tick 1,900; rather, tick 1,900 is when the measurement system is sufficiently confident of the attractor's existence to declare it.

The conflict intensity differential between `bias_calibration_conflict` (max_I = 0.268) and `delayed_context_conflict` (max_I = 0.079) merits examination. The 3.4-fold gap arises not from the number of stressors but from their relationship to the architecture's internal measurement axes. The `delayed_context_conflict` injects a delayed reward and a context switch, both of which affect the `reasoning_vs_fallback` and `reward_vs_energy` axes modestly. The `bias_calibration_conflict` directly attacks the `bias_vs_calibration` axis by simultaneously maximising the numerator and minimising the denominator of the ratio `bias / calibrated_confidence`. The finding that this axis produces disproportionate conflict intensity under direct attack is not surprising architecturally -- it is the intended design: the architecture is most stressed when the two signals most directly in competition (bias and calibration) are simultaneously driven to opposite extremes, and it is least stressed when the competing signals are on different axes with no direct numerator-denominator relationship.

The `delayed_context_conflict` scenario produced no oscillation (`oscillation = False`, `resolved = True`). This is consistent with the theoretical prediction from Dayan and Niv (2008) that delayed reward in the absence of a learning amplifier does not produce oscillation. The delayed feedback creates temporary credit assignment confusion but not sustained strategic switching, because the architecture has no mechanism by which delayed reward feedback propagates back to modify the action selection pathway on a per-tick basis. The confusion resolves passively as the delay buffer catches up to current states.

The sleeping-guard behaviour across all 12 new Day 31 classes is consistent with prior packs. No anomalous updates were recorded in sleeping ticks during the Pack 3 multi-disturbance runs, confirming that the internal `globals()['sleeping'] = True` / `False` management in `MultiDisturbanceScenarioRunner.run()` correctly isolates the test infrastructure's state from the organism's waking-chain updates.

## IX. System Limitations

Several constraints in the Day 31 architecture are identified here for the forward plan.

The 50,000-tick horizon has not been validated. The H5000 run confirms stability over 5,000 ticks, but the behaviour of the 22-class waking chain over 50,000 ticks -- a timescale relevant to real-organism deployment -- is unknown. In particular, the interaction between very long-run bias accumulation and the CRA mechanism over tens of thousands of ticks has not been characterised. Whether the CRA mechanism produces net-positive compression of the error memory or whether it interacts adversarially with sustained low-PE operation to collapse `mean_error` below biologically meaningful thresholds is an open empirical question.

The adversarial and multi-disturbance testing was conducted against a synthetic organism proxy rather than the live `ikigai.py` waking loop. The synthetic proxy correctly implements the mathematical behaviour of the Pack 3 and Pack 4 Day 30 classes but does not reproduce the biological state interactions between cortisol, adenosine, dopamine, and the L2/3 cortical energy system that drive the real organism's decision dynamics. Findings from adversarial testing against the proxy are necessary conditions for claiming robustness but not sufficient ones: an adversarial input that produces no collapse in the proxy may produce collapse in the live organism if it drives the cortisol signal above the sleep-onset gate established in Day 16F or triggers the allostatic alpha mechanism in a way that is not captured by the simplified proxy step function.

The single-file `ikigai.py` architecture, which now contains 26 waking-chain classes plus four adversarial testing classes, four long-horizon analysis classes, and four multi-disturbance testing classes -- 38 classes total in the cognitive and testing layers alone -- is approaching the boundary of maintainability as a single-file system. Circular dependencies between classes are prevented by the sequential waking-chain insertion order, but the absence of module-level imports and explicit API boundaries means that any class can in principle read any global variable at any tick, creating an audit surface that grows quadratically with the number of classes.

The feedback loop from `SystemStabilityMonitor.audit_result` to upstream homeostatic parameters identified in Day 30's limitations section remains unimplemented. The adversarial Pack 1 findings of Day 31 have reinforced the importance of this connection: a system that detects its own failure mode but cannot communicate that detection to the mechanisms that govern sleep onset, cortisol level, or adenosine accumulation has divided its measurement and intervention functions in a way that is architecturally incomplete.

## XII. Scientific Significance -- The Transition from Stable to Validated

Day 30 established that the organism is self-correcting, load-aware, uncertainty-conscious, and habit-forming. Day 31 establishes that those four properties hold when the system is attacked. The distinction is the difference between internal correctness and external robustness -- between a proof in a controlled environment and a demonstration under conditions that the proof did not specify.

The failure discovery in Pack 1 is scientifically significant precisely because it was found, characterised, and corrected. A system that passes every adversarial test on the first pass without any failure discovery is not a robustly tested system; it is an undertested one. The identification of the overconfidence trap as the sole quality=poor scenario in Pack 1 provides direct empirical evidence that the error memory window mechanism in Pack 3 Day 30 -- while correct in design and formula -- exhibits an inertia property that becomes maladaptive under adversarial overconfidence injection. The CRA mechanism corrects this inertia precisely by introducing an active inhibitory decay analogous to the extinction learning mechanisms identified by Bouton (2002) in fear conditioning research, without disturbing the underlying formula or the frozen constants that govern the window's normal operation.

The long-horizon stability result in Pack 2 is scientifically significant because it resolves the open question posed in Day 30 Section VIII: whether the interaction between Pack 3 calibration correction and Pack 4 bias accumulation over thousands of ticks produces low-frequency oscillation in the adjusted confidence signal. The H5000 result -- no oscillation, no bias drift, no lock-in, equilibrium reached and held -- answers this question in the negative for the synthetic proxy case. The architecture's calibration and bias mechanisms do not produce sustained oscillation over 5,000 ticks; they converge to a stable attractor in which the bias is bounded at approximately its baseline value and confidence remains in the `trust_reasoning` regime throughout. This is the long-horizon stability prediction of EEIL confirmed computationally.

The Pack 3 conflict resolution result is scientifically significant because it provides the first direct test of the architecture's calibration-dominance claim under forced opposition. The EEIL cross-environment experiments of Days 20-21 established that alignment without regulation produces degeneration, but those experiments operated through the learning amplifier mechanism in which bias accumulates over many episodes. Pack 3 tests the same structural claim in a single-episode adversarial setting: what happens when bias is instantly amplified to 21 times its baseline value while calibration is simultaneously suppressed by a negative reward? The answer -- the system resolves the conflict correctly, bias is held at its floor, calibration remains operative, and no collapse occurs -- provides a different class of evidence for the same architectural property. Calibration dominance is not only a long-horizon statistical property; it is a tick-by-tick structural property that holds even under direct adversarial forcing of the bias pathway.

## XIII. Day 32 Forward Direction

Day 32 will focus on modular refactoring, architecture migration, and preparation for large-scale validation.

The primary forward objective is a zero-drift modular refactor of `ikigai.py`. The single-file architecture that has served the project from Day 1 through Day 31 has reached a size and complexity that limits both maintainability and the ability to test subsystem interactions in isolation. The Day 31 adversarial and conflict testing infrastructure has demonstrated the value of hermetic class extraction via the experimental exec() loader, but a modular architecture would allow this isolation to be achieved without the loader overhead and without the risk of source-level dependency errors as the class count grows. The refactor target is zero frozen-constant drift: all constants, formulas, and class interfaces must be preserved exactly as documented in Days 1-31. The refactor is a structural reorganisation, not a revision of any frozen law.

The 50,000-tick validation run is deferred to post-refactor. The H5000 result establishes confidence that the architecture is stable in the 5,000-tick regime, but the CRA mechanism's long-run behaviour under extended low-PE operation and the potential for `mean_error` to asymptotically approach zero over tens of thousands of ticks warrant empirical characterisation before the 50,000-tick claim can be made.

The feedback connection from `SystemStabilityMonitor.audit_result` to the homeostatic drive system, identified as an open item in Day 30 and reconfirmed as unimplemented in Day 31, is elevated to a Day 32 objective. The audit verdict is now supported by Pack 1 adversarial stress measurement, Pack 2 long-horizon drift analysis, and Pack 3 conflict resolution analysis -- a richer and more reliable signal than it was at the close of Day 30. Connecting this signal to the sleep-onset probability or cortisol accumulation rate would close the loop between the cognitive audit layer and the biological substrate, implementing the bidirectional measurement-intervention coupling that the architecture's design intends but has not yet wired.
