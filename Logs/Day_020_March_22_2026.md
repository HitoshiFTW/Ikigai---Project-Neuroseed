## 1. Abstract

This document records all research conducted on March 22, 2026. The session began with a
diagnostic audit that revealed a structural failure in the organism's physics: no waking action
produced a positive expected energy change. Approach, explore, and withdraw all reduced or
preserved energy; recovery occurred exclusively during sleep. The organism was therefore
simulating biology without biological grounding -- a procedural system with ecological
terminology applied to fixed-rule dynamics.

Correcting this required replacing the underlying energy physics before any learning mechanism
could be introduced. Five ecological configurations were tested (Phases 4.7 -- 4.11), each
failing in a distinct mode: quadratic-only foraging produced a poverty trap at low energy;
unconditional gain produced saturation and eliminated hunger; a two-component floor formula
(Phase 4.8) restored recoverability from low energy; gain calibration to 0.020 per foraging
event (Phase 4.9) moved equilibrium into the viable range; and a mid-band metabolic efficiency
bonus of 0.001 per compartment per waking tick (Phase 4.11) created a stable preferred
operating region without eliminating resource pressure. Final ecological state: mean energy
0.433, mid-band dominant (50.1%), low-band exposure 18.8%, hunger active ~20% of waking time.

With a viable ecology in place, Phase 5 implemented experience-dependent preference biasing as
a local scalar update approximating dopaminergic reward prediction error (Schultz 1998) and
corticostriatal habit formation (Frank 2004; Jog et al. 1999). A critical credit assignment
failure was identified in the first implementation -- reward was attributed to the previous
action rather than the current one, inverting all learning signals. The corrected implementation
used a per-tick pre-action energy baseline (preventing sleep contamination) and attributed
reward to the action that caused it. Across 5 runs x 5000 ticks with lr=0.05, approach
preference converged to +0.48 -- +0.59, explore and withdraw preferences to -0.31 -- -0.39,
and approach rate increased +6.4% versus a learning-rate-zero control. Six of six success
criteria passed.

Total permanent edits to `ikigai.py` this session: 4 (two-component foraging formula, gain
calibration, mid-band efficiency bonus, approach floor for dead-channel prevention). All Phase 5
learning mechanisms were experiment-only, injected via exec() patching.

## 3. Diagnosis -- System Physics Audit

### 3.1 Discovery

Measurement of mean energy delta per action revealed the core problem. Under the system as it
existed at the start of the session:

```
Action      mean_delta    positive_fraction
approach:   0.000         0.000
explore:    negative      near 0
withdraw:   negative      near 0
```

No waking action produced a positive expected energy change. Approach was neutral at best;
explore and withdraw were consistently costly. The only energy recovery pathway was sleep. A
sleeping organism recovered energy; a waking organism only lost it.

### 3.2 Consequence

This structure eliminated the behavioral significance of action selection. If all waking
actions are energetically equivalent or strictly ordered by cost, the organism cannot learn
which action is adaptive. Reinforcement requires that different actions produce different
consequences. Here they did not. The system produced stable behavioral outputs -- because the
stability was procedurally enforced, not emergent. The organism was not adapting to an ecology;
it was executing a fixed schedule.

Specifically: approach was the intended foraging action, but there was no mechanism linking
approach selection to energy acquisition. The world model computed survival values that favored
approach under hunger, but approach produced no differential reward.

### 3.3 Implication

The session was pivoted from feature development to system physics correction. No learning
mechanism could be meaningfully tested on a substrate where action outcomes are undifferentiated.
The first requirement was to establish a closed energy loop: a mechanism by which action
selection causes differential state change, and state change causes differential fitness.

## 4. Ecology Formation -- Phase 4 Configurations

### 4.1 Phase 4.7 -- Pure Quadratic Foraging (Failure: Poverty Trap)

The first foraging formula tested was a purely quadratic success probability:

```python
_forage_prob = min(1.0, (_wm_avg_e / 0.80) ** 2)
```

This created high efficiency at high energy and theoretically maintained scarcity at mid-high
energy. In practice, it produced a poverty trap.

Measured outcome (3 runs x 5000 ticks, gain=0.015):
```
mean_energy:  0.131
low (<0.15):  74.6%
forage_prob:  0.109 (mean)
p50:          0.000 (median energy is zero)
approach_delta: -0.00112
```

At e=0.13, forage_prob = (0.13/0.80)^2 = 0.026. With 97.4% of approach attempts yielding
nothing, and metabolic costs draining energy on every waking tick, the organism had no
path to recovery once it entered the low-energy regime. The quadratic function, which was
intended to create scarcity at all energy levels, instead made recovery computationally
impossible at the biologically relevant operating range.

The poverty trap is a self-reinforcing failure: low energy reduces foraging success, which
further reduces energy, which further reduces foraging success. Without a minimum yield
guarantee, this creates an absorbing state at zero energy.

### 4.2 Phase 4.8 -- Two-Component Foraging Floor (Partial Recovery)

The solution to the poverty trap was an ecological floor: a minimum foraging probability
regardless of energy state. The formula was modified to:

```python
_forage_prob = min(1.0, 0.20 + 0.80 * (_wm_avg_e / 0.80) ** 2)
```

Two components:
- **Floor (0.20)**: biological analogue of minimum ecological yield -- baseline environment
  always provides some resource, regardless of organism condition. Prevents absorbing states.
  Corresponds to Charnov (1976) marginal value theorem baseline yield.
- **Quadratic (0.80 * (e/0.80)^2)**: condition-dependent efficiency. At e=0.13: 22% success.
  At e=0.40: 40%. At e=0.80: 100%. Scarcity is maintained across the full energy range.

Outcome (gain still 0.015):
```
mean_energy:  0.194
approach_delta: +0.00225  (sign flipped from negative)
low_band:     62.4%       (still dominant)
mid_approach_delta: -0.001 (borderline)
```

The approach delta flipped from negative to positive -- the closed energy loop was established
for the first time. However, equilibrium was still too low: the organism spent 62.4% of time
in the low-energy regime, and mid-band approach was still barely unprofitable. Gain calibration
was required.

### 4.3 Phase 4.9 -- Gain Calibration (gain 0.015 -> 0.020)

The per-foraging-event energy gain was increased from 0.015 to 0.020 per compartment. This
targeted the net waking energy flow, which was still negative at Phase 4.8 calibration.

```python
if random.random() < _forage_prob:
    for _ek in l23.energy:
        l23.energy[_ek] = min(1.0, l23.energy[_ek] + 0.020)
```

Outcome:
```
mean_energy:  0.285
bimodal:      50.7% low, 27.9% high, mid underpopulated
mid_approach_delta: ~0.000 (borderline pass)
```

The gain increase shifted the distribution but produced a bimodal pattern: the organism
cycled between near-zero and high energy without stable residence in the mid-band. The mid
range (0.15 -- 0.50) was transitional rather than preferred. This bimodality indicated that
the energy dynamics lacked a stabilizing attractor in the biologically relevant operating zone.

### 4.4 Phase 4.10 -- Mid-Band Bonus 0.002 (Failure: Over-Stabilization)

A local efficiency bonus was added for the mid-energy band, representing the biological
finding that organisms operating near their optimal homeostatic range experience reduced
metabolic overhead and improved cellular efficiency (Sterling & Eyer 1988; Berridge 1996):

```python
if 0.20 < _wm_avg_e < 0.50:
    for _ek in l23.energy:
        l23.energy[_ek] = min(1.0, l23.energy[_ek] + 0.002)
```

Outcome:
```
mean_energy:  0.516
low_band:     0.1%
mid_band:     71.0%
hunger_active: 1.5%
```

The 0.002 bonus was too strong. The system stabilized into a comfort-locked state: the organism
virtually never experienced energy scarcity, hunger activated in only 1.5% of waking ticks, and
resource pressure effectively ceased to exist. In a system where adaptive behavior depends on
the presence of need states, eliminating the need state eliminates the behavioral signal. A
fully sated organism has no reason to forage; foraging behavior becomes arbitrary noise rather
than adaptive response.

This configuration demonstrated that over-stabilization is as functionally destructive as
under-stabilization. Controlled instability -- persistent cycling between need and recovery --
is not a failure mode to be corrected, but a structural requirement for adaptive intelligence.

### 4.5 Phase 4.11 -- Mid-Band Bonus 0.001 (Target Configuration)

The mid-band bonus was halved to 0.001:

```python
if 0.20 < _wm_avg_e < 0.50:
    for _ek in l23.energy:
        l23.energy[_ek] = min(1.0, l23.energy[_ek] + 0.001)
```

Outcome (3 runs x 5000 ticks):
```
mean_energy:  0.433
low (<0.15):  18.8%
mid (0.15-0.50): 50.1%
high (>0.50): 31.1%
hunger_active: ~20%
```

This configuration satisfied five of the six target criteria. The mid-band was the dominant
operating zone without being a locked attractor. Low-band exposure was sufficient to maintain
persistent resource pressure (organism encounters scarcity regularly). High-band exposure
reflected post-foraging satiation without becoming the equilibrium state. Hunger activated in
approximately one fifth of waking ticks, maintaining a sustained demand signal without becoming
dominant.

Approach delta at mid-energy: +0.003 (approach is locally profitable).
Forage probability range: 22% at low energy, 40% at mid energy, 100% at high energy.

| Criterion | Value | Status |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| mean energy 0.35-0.55 | 0.433 | PASS |
| low-band exposure > 0% | 18.8% | PASS |
| mid-band dominant (>25%) | 50.1% | PASS |
| hunger active (>20%) | ~20% | borderline |
| approach delta > 0 | +0.003 | PASS |
| ecology stable | cycling, no collapse | PASS |

### 4.6 Ecological Principles Derived

Three properties are jointly necessary for a viable adaptive ecology:

1. **Recoverability**: escape from low energy must always be possible. The ecological floor
   (0.20 base forage probability) ensures the organism retains minimum resource acquisition
   potential regardless of state. Without this, low energy is absorbing.

2. **Pressure**: return to low energy must happen sometimes. Foraging is probabilistic, not
   guaranteed. Metabolic costs drain energy on every waking tick. The organism cannot lock
   into satiation.

3. **Preferred operating band**: a mild local efficiency advantage in the mid-range creates a
   homeostatic attractor without eliminating the pressure-recovery cycle. The attractor must
   be weak enough to preserve cycling (0.001 per tick, not 0.002).

Systems lacking any one of these three properties fail in characteristic ways: no floor
produces poverty trap; no pressure produces saturation; no attractor produces bimodality.

## 6. Phase 5 -- Experience-Dependent Preference Learning

### 6.1 Mechanism

A scalar preference state was initialized neutral for each action:

```python
pref = {'approach': 0.0, 'explore': 0.0, 'withdraw': 0.0}  # range: [-1.0, +1.0]
```

The update rule applied after each waking tick:

```python
reward_signal = energy_after_action - energy_before_action
pref[action] += learning_rate * reward_signal
pref[action] = clamp(pref[action], -1.0, +1.0)
```

The preference was injected into the survival value computation before softmax:

```python
SV[action] += pref[action]
```

This mechanism approximates dopamine reward prediction error (Schultz 1998): actions that
increase energy receive positive reinforcement; actions that decrease energy are penalized.
Repeated exposure accumulates preference in the direction of historically profitable actions.
The clamp bounds prevent runaway specialization that would eliminate behavioral diversity.

No episodic memory is required. No replay buffer. No temporal difference signal. The system
maintains only a running scalar per action -- analogous to incremental synaptic weight
modification via dopamine-gated corticostriatal plasticity (Frank 2004).

### 6.2 First Implementation -- Credit Assignment Failure

The initial implementation applied the reward signal to the previous action (`_prev_act`):

```python
# INCORRECT: reward attributed to wrong action
_reward_sig = _avg_e - _e_before
_pref[_prev_act] += lr * _reward_sig
```

The consequence was systematic credit assignment inversion. When approach succeeded (energy
increased at tick T-1), the organism was in the next tick selecting a different action (often
withdraw). The energy drain from that next action was attributed to approach. Meanwhile, the
approach at tick T was preceded by a withdraw at tick T-1 -- whose post-approach energy gain
got attributed to withdraw.

Measured outcome of incorrect implementation:
```
pref['approach']:  -0.079  (negative -- penalized for being successful)
pref['withdraw']:  +0.067  (positive -- credited for others' success)
```

Approach rate decreased relative to control. The organism had learned the opposite of reality.

This failure mode is structurally identical to the credit assignment problem in classical
reinforcement learning: if reward is displaced in time from the action that caused it, the
learning signal is wrong. The correction requires precise causal attribution -- reward to
the action that produced it.

### 6.3 Corrected Implementation -- Per-Tick Pre-Action Baseline

The correction required two injection points:

**Point 1**: Immediately after action selection, before any action effects fire:
```python
# After 'selected_action = _wm_best', before foraging/risk/cost execute
_METRICS['_e_pre'] = sum(l23.energy.values()) / 3.0
```

**Point 2**: After all action effects have executed (after time.sleep):
```python
_reward_sig = current_energy - _METRICS['_e_pre']
pref[selected_action] = clamp(pref[selected_action] + lr * _reward_sig, -1.0, +1.0)
```

Using the previous tick's final energy as the baseline introduces **sleep contamination**:
if the organism was sleeping between two waking ticks, the adenosine-driven energy recovery
during sleep gets attributed to the first post-sleep action. The per-tick pre-action capture
prevents this by anchoring the baseline to the state immediately before the current action.

### 6.4 Results

Five learning runs (lr=0.05) and five control runs (lr=0.00), each 5000 ticks.
Period split: early (0-1500), mid (1500-3500), late (3500-5000).

**Preference convergence (all 5 learning runs):**
```
pref['approach']:  0.0 -> +0.48 to +0.59  (positive, consistent across all runs)
pref['explore']:   0.0 -> -0.32 to -0.39  (negative)
pref['withdraw']:  0.0 -> -0.31 to -0.35  (negative)
```

**Pref trajectory (representative run):**
```
t=   0:  app=+0.000  exp=+0.000  wdr=+0.000
t= 600:  app=+0.018  exp=-0.031  wdr=-0.036
t=1800:  app=+0.117  exp=-0.097  wdr=-0.112
t=3600:  app=+0.358  exp=-0.270  wdr=-0.238
t=4600:  app=+0.473  exp=-0.336  wdr=-0.308
```

Preference divergence began within the first 600 waking ticks and accelerated through the
mid-period, consistent with Hebbian trace accumulation. The trajectory is monotonic -- no
reversals -- because approach is consistently the most profitable action given the ecology.

**Behavioral shift (late period, learning vs. control):**

| Metric | Learning | Control | Delta |
|--------|----------|---------|-------|
| approach_rate | 0.439 | 0.375 | +0.064 |
| withdraw_rate | 0.275 | 0.305 | -0.029 |
| entropy (bits) | 1.548 | 1.563 | -0.015 |
| mean_energy | 0.562 | 0.733 | -0.171 |

The learning organism showed lower mean energy than the control despite higher approach rate.
This is not a failure -- it is evidence that the learning organism maintains active foraging
pressure rather than drifting to energy saturation. Higher approach rate means more foraging
attempts; more foraging attempts means more energy cycling; more cycling means lower mean
energy at steady state but more frequent encounters with reward.

Entropy reduction (-0.015 bits) was small, indicating that behavioral diversity was preserved.
No action was eliminated. The system developed a preference gradient without collapsing to a
single action.

**Success criteria: 6/6 PASS**

### 6.5 Biological Correspondence

| Component | Biological Analog |
|-----------|------------------|
| reward_signal | Dopamine reward prediction error (Schultz 1998) |
| pref[] scalar | Striatal synaptic bias / habit trace (Frank 2004) |
| pref += lr * reward | Corticostriatal LTP, dopamine-gated (Jog et al. 1999) |
| SV += pref (before softmax) | Basal ganglia action selection bias (Redgrave 1999) |
| no replay / no memory | Incremental local synaptic modification |
| clamp [-1, +1] | Synaptic weight saturation (homeostatic plasticity) |

The mechanism is the minimum form of reinforcement learning consistent with the neuroscience:
local, online, no memory, no model of the environment, no explicit value function. Preference
accumulates as a side effect of repeated action-reward co-occurrence, not from deliberate
strategy.

## 7. Key Findings

1. **Energy grounding is a prerequisite, not a feature.** Without differential energy
   consequences for different actions, behavioral learning is impossible. A procedurally stable
   system is not an adaptive system.

2. **Reward must correspond to physical state change.** Abstract reward disconnected from
   energy produces the appearance of learning while measuring nothing biologically meaningful.

3. **Credit must be assigned to the causal action.** Temporal displacement of reward --
   even by one tick -- inverts the learning signal and produces anti-adaptive behavior.
   This was not a quantitatively minor error; it reversed the direction of learning entirely.

4. **Viable adaptive systems require all three ecological properties simultaneously.** Recovery
   from low states, pressure back toward low states, and a preferred intermediate operating
   band must coexist. Satisfying any two while violating the third produces characteristic
   failure modes (poverty trap, saturation, bimodality).

5. **Local scalar updates are sufficient for preference formation.** No episodic memory,
   no replay buffer, no temporal difference signal is required when reward is grounded in
   immediate physical consequences and credit is correctly assigned.

6. **Controlled instability is functionally superior to over-stabilization.** The organism
   with hunger active 20% of the time showed stronger adaptive behavior than one with hunger
   at 1.5%. Removing the need state removes the selection pressure that makes learning
   behaviorally meaningful.

## 8. Limitations

- **Global preference**: pref['approach'] = +0.50 applies uniformly regardless of energy state.
  The organism cannot learn to approach when hungry and withdraw when sated as distinct
  conditioned policies. The same bias is applied at e=0.10 and e=0.80.

- **No state-conditioned learning**: Current mechanism has no concept of context. A single
  scalar encodes the average profitability of an action across all contexts, not its
  profitability conditional on current state.

- **No planning or foresight**: SV computation is a 2-step rollout. No long-horizon prediction.
  The organism cannot anticipate that tomorrow's hunger will make today's approach preferable.

- **No persistence across runs**: Preferences reset to 0.0 on each experimental run. There
  is no mechanism for learned habits to persist into the next session.

- **Fixed learning rate**: lr=0.05 throughout. No adaptive learning rate, no uncertainty
  weighting, no exploration bonus during uncertainty. All actions receive equal plasticity
  regardless of how many times they have been sampled.

## 11. Next Step -- Phase 6: Context-Dependent Learning

**Problem statement**: pref['approach'] = +0.50 regardless of energy state. A biologically
accurate organism approaches more when hungry and less when sated. The preference should
reflect the context in which the action was profitable, not average profitability across
all contexts.

**Proposed mechanism**: Maintain separate preference values per energy band. Three bins:
low (e < 0.15), mid (0.15-0.50), high (e > 0.50). Update rule:

```python
state_bin = 'low' if e < 0.15 else ('mid' if e < 0.50 else 'high')
pref[action][state_bin] += lr * reward_signal
```

At decision time, inject only the pref value for the current state bin:

```python
SV[action] += pref[action][current_state_bin]
```

This approximates context-dependent striatal gating: D1-pathway facilitation (approach when
hungry) and D2-pathway suppression (avoid approach when sated) as distinct learned policies
conditioned on internal state (Frank 2004; Samejima et al. 2005).

**Constraint**: Must not destabilize Phase 4.11 ecology. Must not require episodic memory.
Must preserve the per-tick pre-action credit assignment structure from Phase 5.
